# Frames and Distance

---

## Definition

Frames are structures created with your limbs to manage distance and prevent the opponent from closing space or applying pressure.

Distance is the space between you and your opponent. Frames are the primary tool used to control that space.

---

## Why It Matters

Without frames, you get crushed.

If you cannot manage distance:

- the opponent closes space and applies pressure
- your movement becomes limited
- your breathing becomes restricted
- escapes become much harder

Frames create space. Space creates options.

If this is ignored, the student will constantly accept pressure, get flattened, and lose the ability to move or defend effectively.

---

## Core Idea

Frames create space. Space creates freedom.

---

## Detailed Explanation

Frames are not pushes. They are structures.

A proper frame uses your skeletal alignment to hold space without relying on muscular effort. When done correctly, your bones support the force, not your muscles.

This is what makes frames efficient.

For example, when someone is applying side control, placing your forearm across their neck with your elbow connected to your body creates a frame. If your arm is structured correctly, it prevents them from collapsing their weight fully onto you.

If your arm is bent, disconnected, or weakly positioned, the frame will collapse.

Frames must connect to your body.

A frame that is extended without connection to your hips or core becomes weak. A strong frame links your hands, elbows, and core together into one structure.

Distance is controlled in layers.

- No space → you are being controlled
- Small space → you can begin to move
- Managed space → you can escape or recover position

Frames allow you to move from no space to usable space.

There are different types of frames:

- Direct frames (against the opponent’s body, such as neck or hips)
- Indirect frames (creating space by positioning your limbs between you and the opponent)

Frames are also temporary.

They are not the goal. They are a tool to create space so you can move, recover guard, or improve position.

Holding a frame without using the space it creates is a common beginner mistake.

Another key detail is direction.

Frames must be placed in the correct direction of force. If the opponent is driving forward, your frame must resist that direction. Poorly angled frames collapse even if they look correct.

A student with good framing feels difficult to compress. They maintain space, breathe better, and can begin movement even under pressure.

---

## Key Principles

- Frames rely on structure, not strength
- Keep elbows connected to your body
- Align your frame with the direction of force
- Use frames to create space, not to push endlessly
- Maintain connection between your limbs and core
- Replace collapsed frames immediately
- Use space as soon as it is created

---

## Common Mistakes

- Trying to push instead of frame
- Locking arms straight and losing structure
- Letting elbows drift away from the body
- Holding frames without using the space
- Placing frames in the wrong direction
- Relying on strength instead of alignment
- Allowing frames to collapse without replacing them

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Bottom Positions

- Side control (bottom): frame against the neck and hips to create space
- Mount (bottom): frame to prevent chest pressure and begin escape
- Guard: use frames to maintain distance and prevent passes

### Top Positions

- Recognize and remove opponent frames before advancing
- Collapse frames to close distance and apply pressure

### Transitions

- Use frames to create space before moving
- Transition from frames into guard recovery or escape

---

## Simple Mental Model

Build the frame. Create the space. Use the space.

---

## Optional

### Drills

**Frame Retention Drill**

- Partner applies light pressure
- Maintain frames without collapsing
- Focus on structure and elbow connection

**Frame to Escape Drill**

- Start in side control (bottom)
- Build frames, create space, recover guard
- Repeat continuously

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, frames are used to survive and create space.

At higher levels, frames become more precise:

- combining frames with movement timing
- using frames to off-balance the opponent
- transitioning seamlessly from frames into attacks
