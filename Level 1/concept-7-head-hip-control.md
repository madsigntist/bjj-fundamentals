# Head and Hip Control

---

## Definition

Head and hip control is the ability to control the direction and movement of your opponent by managing their head and hips.

The head determines direction. The hips determine movement.

---

## Why It Matters

If you control the head and hips, you control the person.

If your opponent controls your head and hips:

- Your movement becomes limited
- Your posture breaks
- Your ability to escape is reduced
- You become easier to pin and submit

If you control your opponent’s head and hips:

- You can direct where they move
- You can limit their mobility
- You can stabilize positions
- You can create and maintain control

If this is ignored, the student will focus on grips or limbs while losing control of what actually matters most.

---

## Core Idea

Control the head. Control the hips. Control the opponent.

---

## Detailed Explanation

In jiu-jitsu, not all parts of the body are equal.

The head and hips are the two most important control points.

### Head Control

The head influences direction.

The head dictates the body's direction. If you can turn, pin, or misalign your opponent's head, you can disrupt their posture and control their ability to move effectively.

For example:

- Turning the head away can weaken structure
- Controlling the head can break posture
- Misaligning the head can limit defensive reactions

Losing control of your own head often leads to immediate problems. Your posture breaks, your alignment fails, and your opponent gains advantage.

### Hip Control

The hips generate movement.

Most powerful movements in jiu-jitsu come from the hips. If the hips are free, the opponent can bridge, shrimp, rotate, and escape.

If the hips are controlled:

- Movement is restricted
- Escapes become difficult
- The opponent becomes easier to pin

Controlling the hips often involves:

- Blocking hip movement
- Pinning the hips to the mat
- Controlling angles and rotation

### Combined Control

Controlling only one is not enough.

- If you control the head but not the hips, the opponent can still move
- If you control the hips but not the head, the opponent can still create direction and structure

True control comes from managing both together.

This is what defines strong positions like side control and mount. The opponent’s head is controlled or misaligned, and their hips are restricted.

### Defensive Awareness

On defense, your goal is the opposite:

- Protect your head alignment
- Free your hips

This is the foundation of most escapes.

If your head is controlled and your hips are pinned, you are in a losing position. If you can free your hips or realign your head, you begin to recover.

A student who understands head and hip control starts to see what actually matters in every position. Instead of focusing on surface details, they focus on the true control points.

---

## Key Principles

- The head controls direction
- The hips control movement
- Control both for true dominance
- Protect your own head alignment
- Keep your hips mobile when escaping
- Restrict opponent hip movement when controlling
- Misalign the opponent’s structure whenever possible

---

## Common Mistakes

- Focusing only on grips instead of control points
- Ignoring head position during exchanges
- Allowing hips to move freely when on top
- Trying to control without managing hips
- Forgetting to protect your own head alignment
- Overcommitting to one control point and losing the other
- Not recognizing when hips are free or restricted

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Bottom Positions

- Work to free your hips to create movement
- Protect your head to maintain structure
- Use frames to separate head and hip control

### Top Positions

- In side control, control head direction and pin hips
- In mount, maintain balance while restricting hip movement
- Prevent escapes by managing both control points

### Transitions

- During movement, maintain control of head and hips to stabilize positions
- When losing position, prioritize freeing hips or realigning the head

---

## Simple Mental Model

Control the head. Control the hips.

---

## Optional

### Drills

**Hip Mobility Drill**

- Partner applies light control
- Focus on freeing hips through small movements

**Head Control Awareness Drill**

- Partner attempts to control or turn the head
- Maintain alignment and resist being misdirected

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, focus on recognizing and protecting these control points.

At higher levels, this becomes more advanced:

- Combining head and hip control for stronger pins
- Using misalignment to create attacks
- Controlling transitions through these two anchors
