# Control During Submission

---

## Definition

Control during submission means maintaining dominant positional control throughout the entire finishing sequence: from the initial setup through the isolation, exposure, defense breaking, and final tap. The submission is not a single moment; it is a process that takes time. During that time, the opponent is fighting to escape. If the attacker loses positional control at any point during the process, the submission fails and the position is often lost entirely.

---

## Why It Matters

Submissions are the most dangerous moment for the attacker. This sounds paradoxical: the attacker is on offense, but the reality is that submission attempts require committing the body to the attack, which inherently changes the control position. An armbar from mount requires leaving mount. A triangle from guard requires committing both legs to the choke. A kimura from side control requires releasing the crossface to set the grip. Every submission attempt costs something in positional control.

Without control during submission:

- The opponent escapes the submission and simultaneously improves position: the attacker ends up in a worse position than they started
- Submission attempts become positional gambles rather than controlled finishes
- The attacker hesitates to commit to submissions because they fear losing position: leading to a stalling top game
- Even correct isolation, exposure, and defense breaking are wasted because the opponent escapes during the gap between breaking the defense and finishing

With control during submission:

- The submission attempt is a no-lose situation: either it finishes or the attacker returns to a dominant control position
- The attacker commits fully to the submission because they know their control is secure
- Opponents cannot escape during the finishing sequence because every escape route is closed
- Submission and control are not competing priorities: they work together throughout the process

This is what separates a submission that looks good but fails from a submission that finishes. The technical quality of the choke, armbar, or kimura is the same. The difference is whether the attacker maintained control during every phase of the attack.

---

## Core Idea

The position funds the submission. If the position bankrupts during the attack, the submission cannot cash out. Maintain the account balance the entire time.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**Why control degrades during submissions.** Every submission requires a change from the control position. This change creates gaps:

- **Mount to armbar**: The attacker must swing a leg over the head and lean back. During this transition, the opponent's hips are momentarily free, and the weight that was keeping them flat shifts off center. This gap is where the escape lives.
- **Side control to kimura**: The attacker must release the crossface to grip the arm. Without the crossface, the opponent can turn in and start re-guarding. The crossface was maintaining control; releasing it creates a gap.
- **Back control to rear naked choke**: One arm goes under the chin (the choking arm). That arm was part of the seatbelt. Releasing the seatbelt to choke creates a moment where the back control is weaker.
- **Guard to triangle**: Both legs leave the guard position and lock around the neck and arm. If the triangle is not tight, the opponent can posture up and stack: the attacker's legs are committed and cannot easily return to guard.

The attacker must account for these gaps and close them as part of the submission process, not as an afterthought.

**The three pillars of control during submission.**

_Pillar 1: Lower body anchoring._ The attacker's legs and hips provide the base of control during submissions. Whatever else the upper body is doing (choking, locking, gripping) the lower body must be anchoring.

- In the armbar from mount, the legs squeeze the opponent's torso and head. The knees pinch. The heels hook. The lower body is what prevents the escape while the hands finish the arm
- In the rear naked choke, the hooks (feet inside the thighs) or the body triangle controls the opponent's hips. Without this lower body anchor, the opponent turns out of the choke
- In the triangle, the legs are the submission, but the angle of the hips and the position of the underhook hand provide the stability that prevents the stack

The rule: the lower body controls; the upper body finishes. When students lose submissions, it is almost always because the lower body lost its anchor first.

_Pillar 2: Weight and pressure maintenance._ Even during submissions, the attacker must maintain weight on the opponent. Lifting off to transition to a submission is the primary cause of lost control.

- In the mounted armbar, the attacker must stay heavy during the pivot. Do not rise up before swinging the leg: stay low, keep weight on the chest, and slide the leg over. Rising up allows the opponent to bridge and escape under the lifted weight
- In the side control kimura, maintain chest-to-chest pressure even while setting the grip. Do not lift off to grab the arm: use one hand to set the grip while the body stays heavy. The pressure itself prevents the escape
- In the back choke, maintain chest-to-back pressure. Do not lean away to set the choke: lean in, stay tight, and work the arm under while staying attached

_Pillar 3: Escape route closure._ Before committing to the submission, identify the opponent's most likely escape and close it.

- The opponent's most common armbar escape is bridging and turning toward the attacker. Before locking the armbar, ensure the leg over the head is blocking this turn
- The opponent's most common triangle escape is posturing up and stacking. Before locking the triangle, control the head (pull it down) and angle the hips to prevent the posture
- The opponent's most common back escape is turning into the choker and dropping to the mat. Before committing the choking arm, ensure the hooks or body triangle prevent this turn

The sequence: identify the escape → close the escape route → then commit to the finish.

**The transition tax.** Every submission has a "transition tax": the positional control that is lost during the shift from holding position to attacking the submission. The attacker's job is to minimize this tax.

Ways to minimize the transition tax:

- **Move in stages**: Do not make one explosive transition from control to submission. Break it into stages. From mount, control the arm first (still mounted). Then walk the hips up (still mounted but shifted). Then swing the leg (transitioning). Each stage maintains as much control as possible
- **Maintain one anchor while moving another**: Always have at least one point of heavy control active. If the crossface is released for the kimura, the hip pressure must increase. If a hook is removed for the armbar transition, the other hook must deepen. Something is always holding
- **Use the submission as control**: In many cases, the submission itself contributes to control. A locked triangle controls the posture. A kimura grip controls the arm and prevents the turn. A choking arm under the chin prevents the head from moving. Once the submission is partially set, it begins to serve the control function that was lost during the transition

**When to abort.** If control degrades too much during the submission attempt, the correct response is to abandon the submission and recover the control position. A failed submission attempt from a controlled position is recoverable. A failed submission attempt from a lost position is not.

The decision: "Is my control still sufficient to either finish the submission or return to dominance?" If yes, continue. If no, abandon the submission, rebuild control, and attack again.

---

## Key Principles

- Every submission attempt costs positional control: plan for this cost
- Lower body anchors while upper body finishes
- Maintain weight and pressure throughout the submission process: do not lift off to attack
- Close the opponent's most likely escape route before committing to the finish
- Transition in stages, maintaining at least one anchor at all times
- If control degrades beyond recovery, abandon the submission and rebuild position
- The position funds the submission, never go bankrupt during the attack

---

## Common Mistakes

- Exploding from control to submission in one move: losing all control during the transition
- Lifting off the opponent to set up the submission: creating the space the opponent needs to escape
- Focusing entirely on the upper body attack and letting the lower body go loose: the opponent escapes under the unanchored legs
- Not identifying the opponent's escape route before committing: the opponent uses the obvious escape that was never closed
- Stubbornly holding a submission attempt after control has been lost: the position gets worse with every second
- Treating the submission as a separate phase from control instead of as a controlled continuation of the position

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Armbar from Mount (Controlled Transition)

- **Starting control**: Full mount, chest-to-chest, knees high in armpits
- **Stage 1**: Control the target arm (two-on-one grip, pin to the mat or push toward the head). Hips remain heavy. No transition yet: this is still mount with arm control
- **Stage 2**: Walk the hips slightly toward the head. Shift weight to stay heavy on the chest. The opponent's space to bridge is shrinking, not growing
- **Stage 3**: Swing the leg over the head. This is the highest-risk moment. The bottom leg hooks deep under the opponent's back. The top leg clamps on the head. The knees squeeze. The lower body takes over control as the hips slide off to the side
- **Stage 4**: Finish the armbar with hip elevation. The legs are the control system now: they are squeezing the torso and blocking the turn. The hands finish the arm
- **Escape route closure**: The leg over the head prevents the turn. The bottom hook prevents the bridge-and-slip. If control slips, the attacker can return to mount by swinging the leg back over

### Rear Naked Choke from Back (Controlled Finish)

- **Starting control**: Back control, seatbelt grip, hooks in or body triangle locked
- **Stage 1**: Secure the dominant position. Hooks are deep, seatbelt is tight, the opponent cannot turn or slip down. This is pure Level 3 back control: no submission yet
- **Stage 2**: Begin the hand fight. Use the choking arm to swim under the chin while the free hand strips the opponent's defensive grips. During this phase, the lower body anchor (hooks or body triangle) must remain rock solid: if the hooks slip during the hand fight, the back is lost
- **Stage 3**: Sink the choking arm under the chin. Lock the choke (hand to bicep). The choking arm is now part of the control system: it prevents the opponent from turning out
- **Stage 4**: Squeeze. The lower body prevents hip escape and turning. The chest stays tight to the back. The choke closes
- **Escape route closure**: Hooks prevent dropping the hips. Body position prevents the turn. The choke arm blocks chin tuck. If the choke is not deep enough, maintain back control and re-attempt rather than losing the back for a shallow choke

### Kimura from Side Control (Minimal Transition Tax)

- **Starting control**: Side control, crossface, underhook, chest-to-chest pressure
- **Stage 1**: Shift the underhook to control the near arm. Begin setting the figure-four grip. The crossface stays: do not release it yet. The opponent is still pinned by the head control
- **Stage 2**: Once the kimura grip is locked, the grip itself becomes a control system: it controls the arm and limits the opponent's ability to turn. Now the crossface can be released because the kimura grip has taken over that control function
- **Stage 3**: Step over the head (if needed for finishing angle). Use the near knee to block the opponent's hip from following the step-over. The legs are providing the anchor during the final transition
- **Stage 4**: Rotate the arm to finish. The opponent cannot turn in (knee blocks the hip). The opponent cannot bridge (the kimura grip controls the shoulder). The position is controlled through the finish
- **Escape route closure**: The most common kimura escape is rolling toward the attacker. The knee on the hip prevents this. If the opponent does roll, the attacker can follow and finish from north-south

### Triangle from Guard (Submission as Control)

- **Starting control**: Closed guard, opponent postured down, one arm isolated in and one arm out
- **Stage 1**: Throw the legs up and lock the triangle (one leg over the neck, cut the angle, lock foot behind the knee). This is the highest-risk moment: if the triangle is loose, the opponent postures and stacks
- **Stage 2**: Immediately pull the head down with both hands. This closes the postural escape route. The opponent cannot stack if their head is pulled into their own chest
- **Stage 3**: Adjust the angle. Shift the hips to the side for optimal choking pressure. The triangle is now the control system: it controls the head (trapped between the legs), one arm (inside the triangle), and distance (the opponent cannot posture away)
- **Stage 4**: Squeeze and finish. The locked triangle is simultaneously the submission and the control position. The opponent cannot escape without first defeating the triangle, and defeating the triangle is the same as defending the choke
- **Escape route closure**: Head pull prevents the stack. Angle shift prevents the opponent from reaching over with the free hand. The inside arm is pulled across the body to remove the space it creates. If the triangle is not finishing, maintain the locked position and troubleshoot (angle, tightness, arm position) rather than unlocking and losing control

## Simple Mental Model

A tightrope. The submission is at the other end of the rope. You walk across from your control position to the finish. If you run and lose your balance, you fall off: you lose control and the submission fails. Walk steadily, keep your balance the entire way, and you arrive at the finish without ever losing control. The rope is narrow, but you never have to rush.

## Optional

### Drills

**Staged Transition Drill**

- From mount, practice the armbar transition in four explicit stages, pausing at each stage
- At each pause, the partner tests the control: can they bridge? Can they turn? If yes, the attacker must tighten before advancing to the next stage
- Builds the habit of transitioning in controlled steps, not explosive jumps

**Abort-and-Return Drill**

- Attacker begins a submission from a dominant position
- At any point, the coach calls "Abort"
- The attacker must abandon the submission and return to the original control position cleanly
- If the attacker cannot return to control, the position was gone: meaning the submission would have failed
- Builds the safety net: always have a path back to dominance

**Lower Body Anchor Drill**

- Attacker has back control with hooks in
- Attacker attempts the rear naked choke using only the upper body: the lower body must not change at all
- If the hooks shift, slip, or loosen, the round resets
- Isolates the rule: lower body anchors while upper body finishes

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on understanding that submission attempts cost positional control, and on developing the discipline to transition in stages while maintaining anchors at every step.

At higher levels, control during submission becomes seamless (the attacker moves fluidly from control to submission to finish without any visible transition. The advanced player's submission is indistinguishable from their control) every control position is already a submission setup, and the submission itself is a form of control. The opponent does not experience a gap between "being controlled" and "being submitted": the finish arrives without warning because the attacker never traded control for attack.
