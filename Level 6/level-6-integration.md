# Level 6 Integration - Submission Systems

---

## Level Goal

Finish opponents from controlled positions.

---

## Level Outcomes

- Finish opponents from controlled positions without losing position
- Transition between submissions
- Recognize submission opportunities early

> **IBJJF Competition Note**: Not all submissions are legal at every belt level under IBJJF rules. This curriculum focuses on techniques that are legal within the IBJJF gi ruleset. Straight ankle locks are legal for all belts. Kneebars and toe holds become legal at higher belt levels. Wrist locks are legal from blue belt onward. Always confirm the legality of a technique for your belt level and competition ruleset before applying it in training or competition.

## Concept Summary

| #   | Concept                       | Core Lesson                                                                                                               |
| --- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Isolation Before Attack       | Separate the target from everything that could defend it - remove the help before attacking the limb                      |
| 2   | Exposure Before Finishing     | Position the isolated target so the submission mechanics can work - create the angle, clear the obstruction               |
| 3   | Breaking Defensive Structures | Defeat the opponent's trained defensive grips, postures, frames, and rotations systematically instead of powering through |
| 4   | Control During Submission     | Maintain dominant positional control throughout every phase of the submission - the position funds the attack             |
| 5   | Mechanical Efficiency         | Use hips, legs, and core as the power source - arms and hands are connectors, not the engine                              |

## How the Concepts Connect

Level 6 transforms the submission from a hopeful grab at a limb into a systematic finishing process. The five concepts form a sequential pipeline where each concept addresses a specific phase of the submission, and skipping any phase causes the finish to fail.

**Isolation Before Attack** is the entry gate. No submission works against a connected body. Before anything else, the attacker must separate the target - an arm, the neck, a leg - from the structures that defend it. The free hand, the hips, the posting leg, the defensive posture - all of these must be accounted for. Isolation is the phase that converts a controlled position into a submission opportunity. Without it, the attacker is fighting the opponent's entire body instead of a single vulnerable part.

**Exposure Before Finishing** is the angle. An isolated target is not automatically vulnerable - it must be positioned for the submission to work mechanically. A tucked chin blocks the choke even when the hands are stripped. A bent arm defeats the armbar even when the free hand is controlled. Exposure creates the specific angle, extension, or opening that the submission requires. It converts an isolated target into a mechanically available target.

**Breaking Defensive Structures** is the problem solver. Even after isolation and exposure, the opponent has trained responses: gable grips to block arm extension, chin tucks to block chokes, rolling escapes to change joint angles. These defensive structures are the last line - and they are often strong enough to stop submissions that are otherwise perfectly set up. Structure breaking is the skill that defeats the opponent's practiced defense rather than trying to power through it.

**Control During Submission** is the safety net. Every submission attempt costs positional control - the attacker trades some control to commit to the attack. Control during submission ensures that this trade does not result in a lost position. The attacker transitions in stages, maintains lower body anchors, keeps weight on the opponent, and closes escape routes before committing. This concept makes submission attempts safe - either the submission finishes or the attacker returns to dominance.

**Mechanical Efficiency** is the finishing engine. Once the target is isolated, exposed, defenses broken, and position controlled, the final question is: how is the force applied? Efficient submissions use the hips, core, and chest - not the arms - to generate the finishing force. This produces more force with less effort, allows the submission to be sustained, and makes size advantages irrelevant. Mechanical efficiency is why the same choke can feel effortless from one practitioner and exhausting from another.

The relationship flows like this:

**Isolation Before Attack** (separate the target from help)  
→ **Exposure Before Finishing** (position the target for the submission)  
→→ **Breaking Defensive Structures** (defeat the last active defense)  
→→→ **Control During Submission** (maintain position throughout)  
→→→→ **Mechanical Efficiency** (apply the finish with maximum leverage)

## The Integrated Submission Sequence

When attacking a submission from a controlled position, all five concepts execute together:

1. **Isolate the target** (Isolation) - Identify the target (arm, neck, leg). Control it. Remove the defenders: pin the free hand, block the hips from bridging, prevent the body from turning. Confirm: is the target separated from everything that could help?

2. **Create the exposure** (Exposure) - Position the target for the submission to work. Extend the arm past 90 degrees. Open the throat by lifting the chin. Rotate the heel into position. Clear the structural obstruction that blocks the finish. Confirm: is the target in a position where the submission mechanics can close?

3. **Break the defense** (Structure Breaking) - The opponent defends. Identify the defense type: grip, posture, frame, or rotation. Apply the correct breaking method: two-on-one grip strip, head control to break posture, compression to expel the frame, following or blocking the rotation. Confirm: is the defense defeated? Strip and advance in one motion.

4. **Maintain control** (Control) - Throughout steps 1-3 and during the finish, maintain positional control. Lower body anchors. Weight and pressure. Escape routes closed. If control degrades, abandon the submission and rebuild. The position always funds the attack.

5. **Finish efficiently** (Efficiency) - Apply the submission using the body's strongest muscles. Hips for the armbar. Chest expansion for the choke. Core rotation for the kimura. Hip extension for the straight ankle lock. The arms are connectors. The hips and core are the engine. The finish should feel easy.

## Positional Application

### Rear Naked Choke from Back Control (Full Integration - Strangle)

- **Isolation**: From back control with seatbelt grip and hooks, strip the opponent's defensive grips one at a time. Use the free hand to peel the fingers from the collar. Trap one arm with a hook or by pinning it against the body. The neck is now isolated - one or zero hands are available to defend it.
- **Exposure**: Swim the choking arm under the chin. Use the free hand to lift the forehead and tilt the head back - the throat opens. If the chin is stubborn, wedge the forearm blade under the chin bone and inch it deeper. The neck is now exposed - the forearm is seated, the throat is open.
- **Breaking Defense**: If the opponent re-grips defensively (grabs the choking wrist, reaches for their own collar), break the grip with a two-on-one strip on the weakest hand. If the opponent hunches their shoulders to compress the choke space, drive the hips forward to arch their spine and stretch the neck open.
- **Control**: Throughout the choke sequence, the hooks stay deep in the thighs (or body triangle stays locked). Chest stays tight to the back. The opponent cannot turn, slide down, or create space. If hooks slip during the hand fight, re-secure hooks before continuing the choke.
- **Efficiency**: Lock the choke (hand to bicep). Expand the chest - squeeze the shoulder blades together behind the opponent's head. The force comes from the chest and back, not from arm squeezing. Arch the hips slightly to extend the opponent's neck into the forearm blade. The choke finishes with structural force, not muscular effort.

### Armbar from Mount (Full Integration - Joint Lock)

- **Isolation**: From mount, control the target arm with a two-on-one grip. Pin it to the mat or push it toward the head. Control the free hand - post it away, pin it with a knee, or trap it under a shin. Block the hips from bridging by walking the knees high into the armpits. The arm is now isolated - no hand can help, no bridge can relieve.
- **Exposure**: Walk the wrist grip higher toward the hand to extend the arm. Use the hips as a fulcrum - slight hip elevation pries the arm past 90 degrees. Wedge a knee under the elbow if the arm is stubbornly bent. The arm is now exposed - it is extended past the defensive line and cannot retract.
- **Breaking Defense**: If the opponent locks a gable grip to prevent extension, attack the weak hand with a two-on-one strip. Insert a knee between the gripped hands to wedge them apart. If the opponent hitchhiker-rolls, follow the roll or block it by squeezing the knees and weighting the shoulder.
- **Control**: Transition in stages. Stage 1: control the arm from mount. Stage 2: walk the hips up. Stage 3: swing the leg over the head. At each stage, the lower body anchors - knees squeeze, bottom hook stays deep, top leg clamps the head. If control slips during the pivot, return to mount and re-attempt.
- **Efficiency**: Hands hold the wrist (connectors). The back of the elbow sits on the pelvis (fulcrum). Elevate the hips - hip extension drives the pelvis into the elbow. The finishing force comes from the glutes and hamstrings, not from pulling the arm down with the biceps. The attacker should feel the hip thrust, not the arm pull.

### Triangle from Guard (Full Integration - Strangle)

- **Isolation**: From closed guard, break down the opponent's posture. Overhook one arm and push the other arm across the body - one arm goes inside the triangle, one stays outside. The neck is now between the legs with only one arm to create space. The excluded arm cannot reach inside the choking structure.
- **Exposure**: Lock the triangle (leg over the ankle behind the knee). Angle the hips 30 degrees to one side. This changes the choke line from a straight squeeze to a diagonal cut across both carotid arteries. Pull the head down to remove any remaining posture. The neck is now exposed - positioned at the optimal angle for the blood choke.
- **Breaking Defense**: If the opponent postures and stacks, pull the head down with both hands or sweep the supporting leg. If the opponent inserts a hand inside the triangle to relieve pressure, swim it out or pin it across the body. If the opponent grabs their own hands to resist, break the grip and pull the inside arm across.
- **Control**: Once the triangle is locked, it is simultaneously the submission and the control position. The legs control the head and one arm. The angle controls the posture. The head pull prevents the stack. If the triangle is loose, do not squeeze harder - tighten the lock position first. The triangle must control the opponent before it can finish them.
- **Efficiency**: The finish comes from the angle and the pull, not from thigh squeezing. The hips create the angle (Level 1 force). The arms pull the head (Level 3-4 as connectors). The legs close the structure but do not need to squeeze maximally - the angle does the work. If the choke is not finishing, adjust the angle rather than squeezing harder.

### Kimura from Side Control (Full Integration - Joint Lock)

- **Isolation**: From side control with crossface and underhook, control the near arm. Set the figure-four grip on the wrist. Hold the arm close to the body. The far hand is on the wrong side of the body and cannot easily reach. The opponent is flat under crossface pressure - the arm is isolated from upper body help and the hips are pinned.
- **Exposure**: Paint the arm out - extend it away from the body. Then begin rotating it toward the back. If the opponent's elbow is glued to the ribs, step over to north-south for a better rotation angle. The shoulder is now exposed - the arm is extended and the rotation angle is available.
- **Breaking Defense**: If the opponent grabs their own knee or belt to anchor the arm, use a wedge (insert your knee between the arm and the body) and lever the grip apart. Or lift the elbow first (most grips resist rotation, not elevation) to change the force angle and break the grip. If the opponent rolls toward the attacker, block the roll with a knee on the hip.
- **Control**: Release the crossface only after the kimura grip is set - the grip now controls the arm and prevents the turn. Step over the head for finishing angle. The near knee blocks the hip from following the step-over. The kimura grip + knee on hip + step-over maintains control through the finish.
- **Efficiency**: Lock the grip tight to the body. Rotate the core - turn the torso - and the arm follows. The abdominals and obliques produce the rotational force. The grip is the connector. Walk the legs to shift hip position for additional leverage. The attacker should feel their core working, not their shoulders straining.

### Straight Ankle Lock from Single Leg Entanglement (Full Integration - Joint Lock)

- **Isolation**: From a single leg entanglement entry, the target leg is entangled between the attacker's legs. The opponent's hips are controlled by the leg configuration. The free leg is blocked or managed (controlled with the far leg or pinned by hip positioning). The target leg is isolated - it cannot retract, and the free leg cannot easily assist.
- **Exposure**: Control the foot and position the wrist blade behind the Achilles tendon. Secure the grip by clasping the hands or locking a figure-four around the foot. The ankle is now exposed - the wrist blade is seated against the tendon and the foot is trapped against the chest.
- **Breaking Defense**: If the opponent boots (pushes the attacker's far leg off with their free foot), re-entangle or switch entanglements. If the opponent sits up and reaches for the controlled foot, drive the hips forward to flatten them. If the opponent tries to pull the foot free, squeeze the knees and re-secure the grip.
- **Control**: The legs do all the controlling. The knees pinch the target thigh. The hips press against the opponent's hips. The body position prevents the opponent from sitting up and reaching for the controlled leg. If the entanglement loosens during the exposure or defense-breaking phase, resecure the leg position before attempting the finish.
- **Efficiency**: Hug the foot tight to the chest (connector). Arch backward using hip extension - the same motion as the armbar finish applied to the ankle. The hips drive the wrist blade into the Achilles tendon while the foot is pinned against the chest. The finishing force comes from hip extension (Level 1), not from squeezing or twisting with the arms. The attacker should feel their hips and back working, not their forearms.

## Decision-Making Checklist

When a submission is not finishing, run this diagnostic:

| Check       | Question                                             | If No...                                                                           |
| ----------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Isolation   | Is the target separated from all defenders?          | Identify the defender that is helping and remove it before continuing              |
| Exposure    | Is the target positioned for the submission to work? | Create the angle - extend, rotate, clear the obstruction, or adjust your angle     |
| Defense     | Has the opponent's active defense been broken?       | Identify the defense type and break it - do not try to finish through it           |
| Control     | Do I still have positional control?                  | If control is degraded, abandon the submission and rebuild position                |
| Efficiency  | Am I using the right power source?                   | Redirect force from arms to hips/core/chest - the arms should not be the engine    |
| Power angle | Is my force perpendicular to the lever?              | Adjust the angle of force - diagonal force wastes energy                           |
| Patience    | Am I rushing the finish?                             | Work through the phases in order - isolation → exposure → break → control → finish |

## Drills

**Five-Phase Submission Drill (All Concepts)**

- From a dominant position (mount, side control, or back), execute a submission of choice
- At 30% intensity, complete all five phases explicitly and in order
- Call out each phase as you enter it: "Isolating... Exposing... Breaking defense... Maintaining control... Finishing with hips"
- Partner provides moderate resistance and announces when each phase is successful ("I feel isolated," "The defense is broken")
- Builds the explicit five-phase habit before it becomes automatic

**Submission Diagnostic Drill**

- Attacker attempts a submission and fails
- Both partners freeze in position
- Attacker diagnoses the failure using the Decision-Making Checklist: "Isolation was good. Exposure was incomplete - the arm is still bent. I need to extend before trying to finish."
- Reset from the same position and address the identified gap
- Builds analytical thinking about submission failures

**Efficiency Comparison Drill**

- Execute the same submission twice: once using only arms and grip (deliberately inefficient), once using hips and core (efficient)
- Note the difference in effort, force, and finishing speed
- Develops awareness of the difference between efficient and inefficient mechanics by feeling both

**Strangle and Lock Circuit**

- 5-minute rounds. Attacker cycles through four submissions: rear naked choke, armbar, triangle, kimura
- For each one, complete the full five-phase sequence
- If isolation fails, the attacker must return to control position and try a different submission
- If a defense holds for 15 seconds despite correct isolation and exposure, transition to the next submission
- Builds fluency across submission types and the habit of transitioning between attacks

**Position-to-Submission Flow Drill**

- Start from a passing position. Pass the guard (Level 5) → establish control (Level 3) → execute a submission (Level 6)
- The full chain: pass → control → isolate → expose → break → maintain → finish
- Builds the connection between passing, control, and submission as one continuous system

## Relationship to Other Levels

**Level 1 (Mechanical Foundations)** provides the structural tools that submissions exploit and that defenders use. Frames are both the attacker's lever (the forearm blade in a choke is a frame) and the defender's barrier (a forearm frame inside a triangle). Posture is what the attacker breaks to expose the neck and what the defender uses to resist. Understanding Level 1 from both sides makes submission offense and defense intelligible.

**Level 2 (Defense and Escape)** is the opponent's trained response to Level 6. Elbow-knee connection, frame creation, alignment under pressure, guard recovery - all of these are what the opponent uses to survive and escape submissions. Breaking Defensive Structures (Concept 3) is a direct counter to Level 2 defenses. Understanding what the opponent learned in Level 2 tells the attacker exactly what defense they will face and how to defeat it.

**Level 3 (Positional Control)** is the starting point for every submission. Submissions happen from controlled positions - side control, mount, back control. Level 3's pinning mechanics, weight distribution, and connection and pressure create the platform from which submissions launch. Control During Submission (Concept 4) is the continuation of Level 3 principles into the attacking phase - the same skills that hold position now hold position while attacking.

**Level 4 (Guard Systems)** provides submission opportunities from bottom. The sweep-vs-submission decision framework (Level 4, Concept 6) is the entry point - when the opponent's posture breaks, submissions become available from guard. Triangle, armbar, omoplata, and guillotine from guard all follow the Level 6 sequence: isolate, expose, break defense, maintain control, finish efficiently. Guard submissions require guard retention (Level 4) as the control system rather than top control (Level 3).

**Level 5 (Passing Systems)** connects directly to submissions through the pass-to-control-to-submission chain. Frame clearing during passing (Level 5, Concept 4) often exposes submission targets - a stripped arm during a pass is an isolated arm for a submission. Pressure passing fatigues the opponent, setting up submissions by degrading their defensive energy. The pass is not the end; it is the opening for Level 6.

**Level 7 (System Integration)** takes Level 6 submissions and chains them into attack sequences. Dilemma creation uses multiple submissions at once - defend the armbar, expose the triangle. Chaining attacks uses Level 6's transition-between-submissions outcome. Level 6 provides the individual submissions; Level 7 connects them into an inescapable system.

## Submission Categories

Level 6 applies equally to both major submission categories:

**Strangles** (blood chokes and air chokes): Rear naked choke, guillotine, triangle, arm triangle, ezekiel, loop choke, baseball bat choke, bow and arrow. All follow the five-phase sequence. Isolation targets the neck. Exposure opens the throat. Defense breaking strips the hands from the collar or breaks posture. Control maintains back control, mount, or guard during the attack. Efficiency uses chest expansion, hip extension, or positional angle rather than arm squeezing.

**Joint Locks** (hyperextension and rotation): Armbar, kimura, americana, omoplata, straight ankle lock, kneebar, toe hold, wristlock. All follow the five-phase sequence. Isolation targets the joint. Exposure extends or rotates the limb past its defensive line. Defense breaking strips anchor grips and defeats rolling escapes. Control maintains position during the transition to the lock. Efficiency uses hip elevation, core rotation, or trunk rotation rather than arm strength.

The five concepts apply identically to both categories. The specific techniques differ; the principles do not.

## Summary

Level 6 is a complete submission system. Isolation separates the target from help. Exposure positions the target for the finish. Breaking defensive structures defeats the opponent's trained last-line defense. Control during submission ensures the attacker never loses position during the attack. Mechanical efficiency applies the finish using the body's strongest muscles with maximum leverage.

The submission does not succeed because of one explosive grab. It succeeds because the attacker executes a systematic sequence - isolating, exposing, breaking, controlling, and finishing - where each phase makes the next phase possible and skipping any phase causes the whole system to fail. When all five concepts work together, the finish feels inevitable to the defender and effortless to the attacker.
