# Frame Creation and Maintenance

---

## Definition

Frame creation and maintenance is the practice of building skeletal structures with your limbs to manage space, and keeping those structures intact as the opponent applies pressure and attempts to remove them.

At this level, framing moves beyond basic awareness into deliberate, repeated use under resistance.

---

## Why It Matters

A frame that collapses is the same as no frame at all.

In Level 1, the concept of frames was introduced as a tool for creating space. At this level, the focus shifts to building frames under pressure and keeping them functional when the opponent is actively trying to break them down.

Without frame maintenance:

- the opponent closes space and re-establishes control after every small gain you make
- escapes stall halfway through because the structure holding your space disappears
- you burn energy rebuilding frames that should have been maintained
- the opponent learns that your frames are temporary and stops respecting them

Good frame maintenance turns a single effort into lasting advantage. Poor frame maintenance turns every escape into a cycle of building, losing, and rebuilding.

---

## Core Idea

A frame only matters if it stays.

---

## Detailed Explanation

Creating a frame is the first step. Maintaining it is where the real skill lives.

When you are underneath an opponent, you have limited resources. Your arms, your legs, your hips, your head. Frames use these resources to carve out space between you and the person controlling you. But the opponent knows this. Their job is to collapse your frames, flatten you, and remove the space you create.

This turns framing into a contest.

You build structure. They attack it. You must defend your frames the same way you defend your position.

Frame creation under pressure requires timing. There are moments when the opponent's weight shifts, when their grip changes, or when they transition between attacks. These moments are windows. A frame inserted during a window is much easier to establish than one forced against full resistance.

Once the frame is in place, maintenance becomes the priority.

Maintenance means keeping your elbows connected to your body, keeping your wrists aligned with your forearms, and keeping your frame angled against the direction of force. If any of these connections break, the frame weakens.

There are several layers to frame maintenance:

**Reconnection.** When a frame is partially collapsed, it must be rebuilt immediately. Waiting allows the opponent to settle deeper. The longer you wait, the harder reconstruction becomes.

**Replacement.** Sometimes a frame cannot be saved. When one frame is stripped, another must take its place before the space closes. This requires awareness of which limbs are available and where the next frame should go.

**Reinforcement.** Two connected frames are stronger than one isolated frame. When possible, frames should support each other. A forearm frame against the neck becomes stronger when your other hand grips your own wrist. A knee shield becomes stronger when paired with a hand frame on the bicep.

One of the most overlooked aspects of frame maintenance is body position. A frame connected to a body that is flat on its back is weaker than the same frame connected to a body that is turned on its side. Your overall alignment feeds into how strong your frames can be.

Frames are not walls. They do not hold space indefinitely on their own. They hold space long enough for you to act. The maintenance keeps the window open. What you do with that window is the next step.

---

## Key Principles

- Create frames during windows of opportunity, not against full pressure
- Keep elbows connected to your body at all times
- Align frames against the direction of the opponent's force
- Replace collapsed frames immediately - do not pause
- Reinforce frames by connecting two structures together when possible
- Body position supports frame strength - stay on your side when underneath
- Frames hold space temporarily - use the space before it closes

---

## Common Mistakes

- Building frames too late, after the opponent has fully settled
- Letting elbows drift away from the body, weakening the structure
- Using muscular effort instead of skeletal alignment
- Holding a frame without acting on the space it creates
- Allowing a collapsed frame to stay collapsed instead of rebuilding or replacing
- Framing in the wrong direction relative to the opponent's pressure
- Ignoring body position - staying flat while trying to frame

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Bottom Positions

- Side control (bottom): frame against the neck with your near-side forearm, frame against the hip with your far-side hand - maintain both while working to your side
- Mount (bottom): frame across the chest or against the hips to prevent the opponent from climbing high - maintain while setting up bridge and escape
- Half guard (bottom): use the knee shield as a primary frame, reinforce with a hand on the bicep - maintain while working for underhook or guard recovery

### Top Positions

- Recognize which frames the bottom player is relying on
- Attack the weakest frame first - collapsing one often causes the others to fail
- Settle weight after removing a frame to prevent reconstruction

### Transitions

- During guard recovery: frames buy the time needed to reinsert legs and rebuild guard
- During escapes: the frame that holds longest determines whether the escape succeeds

---

## Simple Mental Model

Build it. Keep it. Use it. If it breaks, build the next one immediately.

---

## Optional

### Drills

**Frame Maintenance Under Pressure**

- Start in side control bottom with frames already in place
- Partner applies increasing pressure
- Goal is to maintain frames for 30 seconds without collapsing
- Focus on alignment, elbow connection, and breathing

**Frame Replacement Drill**

- Partner strips one frame from side control or mount
- Immediately replace it with another structure
- Repeat continuously for rounds of 60 seconds
- Build the habit of never accepting zero frames

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on creating frames deliberately and keeping them alive under pressure.

At higher levels, frame creation becomes faster and more instinctive. Frames begin to combine with movement - creating space and escaping in one motion rather than two separate steps. Frame maintenance also becomes more subtle, using small adjustments instead of full reconstructions.
