# Hand Fighting Basics

---

## Definition

Hand fighting is the practice of controlling, redirecting, stripping, and establishing grips using your hands and wrists to gain positional advantage.

It is the battle that happens before and during every position: the contest over who controls the connection points between two people.

---

## Why It Matters

Grips dictate control. Whoever wins the grip exchange usually wins the next position.

Without hand fighting:

- the opponent establishes grips freely and controls the pace
- you react to their control instead of building your own
- frames are stripped without resistance
- escapes are blocked because you never addressed the hands holding you in place

Hand fighting is not flashy. It does not get attention. But it is the layer that makes everything else work. A perfect hip escape fails if the opponent has an uncontested grip on your head. A strong frame collapses if you never dealt with the hand that is stripping it.

If this is ignored, the student is always one step behind. They are defending a position that was lost the moment they lost the grip exchange.

---

## Core Idea

Control the hands, control the fight.

---

## Detailed Explanation

Hand fighting is the first contest in every exchange.

Before a pass happens, there is a grip battle. Before a submission is locked, there is a grip battle. Before mount is established, there is a grip battle. Before a sweep works, there is a grip battle.

At this level, hand fighting is about three basic skills.

**Grip prevention.** The easiest grip to deal with is the one that never gets established. When you see the opponent reaching for your collar, your wrist, your ankle, or your head, the first option is to prevent it. A quick redirect, a push on their hand, turning your body to change the angle: anything that stops the grip from landing.

Prevention costs very little energy. Dealing with an established grip costs a lot.

**Grip stripping.** When a grip is established, it must be removed before you can move effectively. Stripping grips is not about yanking. It is about understanding how the hand is connected and breaking that connection at the weakest point.

Most grips break at the fingers. Pulling against the thumb side of a grip is easier than pulling against the fingers. Peeling a grip with two hands against their one is efficient. Redirecting the grip by pushing the elbow rather than fighting the hand directly can be more effective than brute force.

Grip stripping must have purpose. You do not strip a grip just to be free. You strip it to create a window for your next action: a frame, an escape, a guard recovery, or a positional change.

**Grip establishment.** Once you have dealt with the opponent's grips, you need to build your own. Grips serve frames, sweeps, escapes, and control.

At this level, important grips to understand include:

- Wrist control: controlling the opponent's hand to prevent them from posting, gripping, or framing
- Collar or head control: affecting posture and direction
- Sleeve or elbow control: managing the opponent's arm position and preventing crossface
- Ankle or pant control: relevant in guard positions for distance management

The grip you choose depends on the position and your goal. There is no universal best grip. The best grip is the one that serves your next action.

Hand fighting also has a timing component.

Grips change constantly. What you control now may be stripped in two seconds. What the opponent controls may shift as they adjust. Effective hand fighting is not about winning once. It is about staying engaged: constantly adjusting, re-gripping, stripping, and re-establishing as the position evolves.

This is where most beginners struggle. They fight for a grip, get it, and then stop. They hold static while the opponent continues to work. The grip becomes stale, the position changes around it, and the advantage disappears.

Hand fighting is continuous.

---

## Key Principles

- Preventing a grip is easier than stripping one
- Strip grips at the weakest point, usually the thumb side
- Use two hands against one when stripping an established grip
- Every grip strip should lead to your next action
- Establish grips that serve your immediate positional goal
- Hand fighting is continuous: do not stop after winning one exchange
- Your hands should always be doing something purposeful

---

## Common Mistakes

- Allowing the opponent to establish grips without resistance
- Fighting grips with strength instead of technique and angles
- Stripping a grip and then pausing with no follow-up action
- Holding the same grip for too long without adjusting to the changing position
- Ignoring hand fighting in bottom positions and only focusing on it standing or in guard
- Using both hands to fight one grip while the opponent's other hand works freely
- Not recognizing which grips are dangerous and which are manageable

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Bottom Positions

- Side control (bottom): fight the crossface hand before it controls your head. Strip it, redirect it, or frame against it
- Mount (bottom): control the opponent's wrists to prevent them from establishing collar grips or posting for balance
- Guard (bottom): establish sleeve and collar grips to set up sweeps and maintain distance

### Top Positions

- Establish grips before advancing: control the head, control a wrist, pin a shoulder
- Strip the bottom player's frames by attacking their grip points
- Hand fighting from top is about removing the opponent's tools one at a time

### Transitions

- During guard passing, the grip exchange often determines whether the pass succeeds
- During escapes, stripping a single controlling grip can be the difference between staying pinned and getting free

---

## Simple Mental Model

Their grips are their control. Remove them.

Your grips are your control. Establish them.

Never let your hands be idle.

---

## Optional

### Drills

**Grip Strip Drill**

- Partner establishes grips from various positions (collar, wrist, sleeve)
- Strip each grip as efficiently as possible
- Focus on technique: thumb side, two-on-one, elbow push
- No positional movement, just grip work in isolation

**Grip Fighting Rounds**

- From seated or kneeling position facing each other
- Both players fight to establish and maintain grips for 60-second rounds
- No takedowns or sweeps: only grip work
- Builds awareness of the constant exchange

**Grip to Action Drill**

- Strip a grip and immediately perform a follow-up action (frame, hip escape, guard recovery)
- Builds the habit of connecting grip stripping to purpose
- Repeat from multiple positions

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on recognizing grip exchanges, learning to strip basic grips, and understanding that hand fighting is a constant part of every position.

At higher levels, hand fighting becomes more strategic. Students learn specific grip sequences for particular positions, develop the ability to read the opponent's intentions through their grip choices, and begin using grip fighting offensively to set up attacks rather than only defensively to clear threats.
