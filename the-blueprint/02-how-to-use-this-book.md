# How to Use This Book

---

## Reading Order

This book is designed to be read in order — Level 1 through Level 7, front to back. Each level depends on the concepts introduced in the levels before it. Skipping ahead will not ruin anything, but it will reduce comprehension. Level 5 references Level 3 principles constantly. Level 7 references everything.

If you are a complete beginner, start at Level 1 and work forward.

If you are an intermediate practitioner, you may be tempted to skip the early levels. Do not. Read them anyway. You will likely discover that concepts you thought you understood have gaps — and those gaps explain problems in your current game. The early levels are fast reads for experienced students, but they are not empty reads.

If you are an instructor, read the entire book once before teaching from it. The curriculum is designed as a system, and understanding the destination (Level 7) will change how you teach the beginning (Level 1).

## Structure of Each Level

Every level contains three types of content:

### Concept Sections

Each concept is a standalone section that follows a consistent ten-part structure:

1. **Definition** — What the concept is, in one to two sentences
2. **Why It Matters** — What goes wrong without it
3. **Core Idea** — The essential principle distilled to its simplest form
4. **Detailed Explanation** — The full breakdown with mechanics and reasoning
5. **Key Principles** — The rules that govern the concept
6. **Common Mistakes** — What students get wrong and why
7. **Application / Positional Examples** — How the concept looks in specific positions
8. **Simple Mental Model** — A metaphor or image that makes the concept stick
9. **Drills** — Practice methods (optional within the concept section; all drills are compiled in the Drill Index)
10. **Progression Notes** — How this concept connects to other levels

This structure means you can read any concept section and walk away with a complete understanding of that idea — its theory, its application, its pitfalls, and its connections.

### Integration Section

Each level ends with an integration section that ties the level's concepts together. It includes:

- **Level Goal and Outcomes** — What the student should be able to do after this level
- **Concept Summary Table** — All concepts at a glance
- **How the Concepts Connect** — A narrative explanation of how the concepts feed each other, with a flow diagram
- **The Integrated Sequence** — Step-by-step execution combining all concepts
- **Positional Application** — Full worked examples in specific positions
- **Decision-Making Checklist** — A table of questions to ask and actions to take
- **Drills** — Practice methods that integrate all concepts simultaneously
- **Relationship to Other Levels** — Where this level fits in the curriculum

The integration section is where individual concepts become a system. Read the concepts first, then read the integration to see how they combine.

## How to Practice

Reading is necessary but not sufficient. This is a physical discipline, and the concepts only become real through practice.

Reading a concept and owning it are separated by mat time. Understanding how off-balancing works on the page is nothing like executing it against a fully resisting opponent. The bridge between the two is **live training** — drilling and sparring against partners who resist, counter, and impose their own game. What you practice is what you produce under pressure. If your training consists only of cooperative drilling with a passive partner, your ability to apply concepts under genuine pressure will remain theoretical. Live training against resisting partners develops the attributes — timing, recognition, composure, speed of application — that make concepts functional.

Here is the recommended approach:

### Phase 1: Read and Understand

Read one concept section. Make sure you understand the core idea, the key principles, and the common mistakes. If you can explain the concept in your own words without looking at the page, you understand it.

### Phase 2: Isolate and Drill

Practice the concept in isolation using the drills provided. This means positional drilling with a cooperative partner at low resistance. The goal is to feel the concept — to experience the difference between doing it correctly and doing it incorrectly.

### Phase 3: Integrate Within the Level

After all concepts in a level are understood individually, practice the integration drills from the level's integration section. These drills combine multiple concepts and require the student to use them together.

### Phase 4: Apply in Live Rolling

Take the concepts into live sparring. Start with positional sparring (specific situations with defined starting positions) before going to full open rolling. Focus on one or two concepts per session — do not try to apply everything at once.

### Phase 5: Diagnose and Return

When something fails in live rolling, diagnose the failure. Which concept broke down? Return to that concept's chapter, re-read it, and drill the specific area that failed. This feedback loop — practice, fail, diagnose, return, re-drill — is where real learning happens.

## Pacing

There is no fixed timeline. Some students will spend two weeks on a level. Some will spend two months. The curriculum responds to the student's pace, not the other way around.

The guideline is: do not move to the next level until you can execute the current level's integration drills at moderate resistance without thinking about what comes next. The concepts should feel natural before you add more.

A reasonable estimate for a practitioner training three times per week:

- Levels 1-2: 4-8 weeks each
- Levels 3-4: 6-10 weeks each
- Levels 5-6: 8-12 weeks each
- Level 7: Ongoing — system integration is a career-long practice

## What "Concept-First" Means in Practice

When this book says "armbar from mount," it is not teaching you a technique. It is using a familiar technique to illustrate a concept — such as "isolation before attack" (Level 6, Concept 1). The armbar is an example. The concept is the lesson.

This means:

- Do not memorize the specific technique examples as the primary content. Memorize the concept.
- When you encounter a position not explicitly covered in the book, apply the relevant concepts from the appropriate level. The concepts are universal; the positions are just contexts.
- If a technique you already know contradicts a concept, investigate — either your understanding of the concept is incomplete, or your execution of the technique includes unnecessary compensations.

## Reference Materials

The back of this book contains several reference sections designed for ongoing use:

- **Positional Reference Guide** — Every major position broken down using a consistent template
- **Drill Index** — All 32 drills from the curriculum in one place, organized by level and type
- **Training Plan** — A suggested schedule for working through the curriculum
- **Glossary** — Definitions for all BJJ terminology used in the book
- **Quick Reference Sheets** — Condensed versions of key frameworks and checklists
- **Troubleshooting Guide** — Common problems mapped to specific concepts and levels

These are designed to be returned to repeatedly. Dog-ear them, bookmark them, photocopy them and pin them to the wall. They are the practical tools that support the conceptual material.
