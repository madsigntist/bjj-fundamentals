# Dilemma Creation

---

## Definition

Dilemma creation means presenting the opponent with two or more simultaneous threats that cannot all be defended at the same time. When the opponent defends one threat, the other becomes available. When they switch to defend the second, the first reopens. The opponent is forced to choose which threat to give up - and either choice results in the attacker gaining something.

---

## Why It Matters

A single threat can always be defended. One armbar attempt - the opponent knows what is coming, puts both hands on it, and defends with their full attention and body. One sweep attempt - the opponent bases, widens, and shuts it down. Single threats are predictable, and predictable attacks fail against trained opponents.

Without dilemma creation:

- Every attack is a single isolated event that the opponent can focus their entire defense on
- The opponent is never forced to make a bad choice - they just defend the one thing
- As opponents get better, single attacks have lower and lower completion rates
- The attacker works harder and harder for diminishing returns - effort goes up, finishes go down

With dilemma creation:

- The opponent cannot focus on one threat because doing so opens another
- Every defensive choice is a sacrifice - defending A gives up B
- The attacker does not need to outmuscle or outspeed the defense - they outthink it
- Finishing rates increase because the opponent is defending the wrong thing when the real attack arrives
- The exchange is controlled by the attacker because they set the terms of the dilemma

This is the concept that separates systematic jiu jitsu from random attacking. Levels 1-6 gave the student individual skills - survival, escape, control, guard, passing, submissions. Level 7 begins by connecting those skills into combinations where the whole is greater than the parts.

---

## Core Idea

Make the opponent choose between two bad options. Whichever they choose, you take the other.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**The structure of a dilemma.** Every dilemma has three components:

1. **Threat A**: The first attack or positional advance
2. **Threat B**: The second attack or positional advance, which becomes available when the opponent defends Threat A
3. **The fork**: The point where the opponent must choose which threat to address - they cannot address both simultaneously

The attacker controls the fork. They present Threat A knowing the opponent will defend it. The defense of Threat A creates the opening for Threat B. The attacker is not surprised by the defense - they planned for it.

**Types of dilemmas.**

_Position vs submission._ The opponent must choose between giving up position or giving up a limb/neck. Example: from mount, threaten an armbar. The opponent turns to their side to defend (taking the arm away). The turn exposes the back. The dilemma: stay flat and lose the arm, or turn and lose the back. Either choice gives the attacker something.

_Submission vs submission._ Two submissions are available simultaneously, and defending one opens the other. Example: from closed guard with a broken posture, threaten the triangle. The opponent pulls the arm out to defend (straightening the arm). The straightened arm is available for an armbar. The dilemma: keep the arm in and get triangled, or pull the arm out and get armbarred.

_Sweep vs submission._ The opponent must choose between being swept or being submitted. Example: from butterfly guard with an underhook, threaten the hook sweep. The opponent bases wide and posts to prevent the sweep. The posting arm is now committed and cannot defend - set up a guillotine or arm drag. The dilemma: keep the base narrow and get swept, or base wide and expose the arm or neck.

_Pass vs pass._ Two passing directions are available, and defending one opens the other. Example: from standing, threaten a torreando to the left. The opponent blocks by turning their hips left. The hips are now turned - pass right with a leg drag. The dilemma: block the torreando and give up the leg drag, or stay neutral and get torreandoed.

**How to construct a dilemma.** The attacker does not stumble into dilemmas - they build them deliberately:

1. **Choose a primary threat** - the attack you want to land or the attack that is easiest to threaten from the current position
2. **Identify the most likely defense** - what will the opponent do when they see this threat? Trained opponents have predictable defensive responses
3. **Identify what the defense opens** - when the opponent defends the primary threat, what becomes vulnerable? What position does their defense create?
4. **Prepare the secondary threat** - set up the grips, positioning, or angle needed to exploit what the defense opens
5. **Present the primary threat** - commit enough that the opponent must respond
6. **Transition to the secondary threat** - when the defense arrives, take what it gives you

The key is step 2: knowing the most likely defense. This is not guessing - it is understanding the opponent's options. From mount, an armbar defense almost always involves turning to the side. From closed guard, a triangle defense almost always involves pulling the arm out. These are the logically best defensive responses, and trained opponents will reliably choose them. The dilemma is built on this predictability.

**Dilemmas from every level.**

- **Level 3 (Control)**: From side control, threaten the mount transition. The opponent frames and hip escapes to prevent it. The frame exposes the arm - set up a kimura. Control dilemma: stop the mount or protect the arm.
- **Level 4 (Guard)**: From closed guard, threaten a hip bump sweep. The opponent posts a hand to prevent the sweep. The posted arm is extended - armbar on the posted arm. Guard dilemma: get swept or get armbarred.
- **Level 5 (Passing)**: Threaten a pressure pass (knee cut). The opponent hip escapes to create distance. Take the back from the hip escape. Passing dilemma: accept the pressure or give the back.
- **Level 6 (Submissions)**: From back control, threaten the rear naked choke. The opponent defends the neck by tucking the chin and using both hands. Both arms are committed to defense - transition to an armbar on the defending arm. Submission dilemma: protect the neck or protect the arm.

**Dilemma stacking.** Advanced dilemma creation layers multiple forks:

- Threat A forces a defense that opens Threat B
- Threat B forces a defense that opens Threat C
- Threat C forces a defense that opens Threat A again

The opponent is caught in a cycle where every defense creates the next problem. This is the foundation of attack chains (Concept 2) and is what makes high-level jiu jitsu feel inescapable.

---

## Key Principles

- A single threat can always be defended - dilemmas force the opponent to choose which threat to concede
- Every dilemma has three components: Threat A, Threat B, and the fork where the opponent must choose
- The attacker controls the fork by knowing the most likely defense to Threat A
- Dilemmas exist at every level: position vs submission, submission vs submission, sweep vs submission, pass vs pass
- The defense to one threat is the opening for the next - this is by design, not by accident
- Dilemma stacking creates cycles where every defense feeds the next attack
- Construction is deliberate: choose the primary threat, predict the defense, prepare the secondary threat

---

## Common Mistakes

- Attacking with a single threat and hoping it works - no secondary plan when the defense arrives
- Not predicting the defense - being surprised when the opponent defends, instead of planning for it
- Setting up Threat A without preparing Threat B - the secondary threat is not ready when the opening appears
- Committing too fully to Threat A, making it impossible to transition to Threat B when the defense comes
- Presenting both threats too obviously - the opponent reads the dilemma and finds a third option that defends both
- Not recognizing natural dilemmas that already exist in common positions - every position has built-in forks

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Mount - Armbar vs Back Take

- **Threat A**: Isolate the arm and begin the armbar setup from mount
- **Predicted defense**: Opponent turns to their side to protect the arm, curling the elbow to the body
- **What the defense opens**: The turn exposes the back - the far hip lifts, the near arm becomes the underhook point
- **Threat B**: Abandon the armbar, take the back from the turn - insert hooks as the opponent rolls to their side
- **The fork**: Stay flat and lose the arm, or turn and lose the back

### Closed Guard - Triangle vs Armbar

- **Threat A**: Break posture, overhook the arm, throw the legs up for a triangle
- **Predicted defense**: Opponent pulls the trapped arm out of the triangle (the standard triangle defense)
- **What the defense opens**: The arm is straightened and extended during the pull - it is exposed for an armbar
- **Threat B**: As the arm pulls free, catch it with both hands, pivot the hips, and lock the armbar on the extending arm
- **The fork**: Keep the arm in and get triangled, or pull the arm out and get armbarred

### Side Control - Mount vs Kimura

- **Threat A**: Begin transitioning to mount - slide the knee across the hip
- **Predicted defense**: Opponent frames against the knee and hip escapes to prevent the mount
- **What the defense opens**: The framing arm is extended and committed - the elbow is away from the body
- **Threat B**: Catch the framing arm with a kimura grip - the arm that was defending the mount is now isolated
- **The fork**: Accept the mount or expose the arm

### Butterfly Guard - Sweep vs Guillotine

- **Threat A**: Underhook plus butterfly hook - threaten the hook sweep
- **Predicted defense**: Opponent posts wide and bases hard to prevent being swept
- **What the defense opens**: The wide post commits both arms to the mat - the head drops as the opponent bases forward
- **Threat B**: Snap the head down into a guillotine - the posted arms cannot defend the neck
- **The fork**: Stay light and get swept, or base hard and give the neck

---

## Simple Mental Model

A fork in the road. You are herding the opponent down a path, and the path splits. Left goes to an armbar. Right goes to a back take. You do not care which way they turn - both roads end badly for them. Your job is to herd them to the fork. Their "choice" is an illusion.

## Optional

### Drills

**Dilemma Identification Drill**

- From any dominant position, identify one natural dilemma (two threats where defending one opens the other)
- Write down: Threat A, predicted defense, what the defense opens, Threat B
- Practice the sequence at 50% - present A, wait for the defense, take B
- Repeat from 5 different positions to build a library of dilemmas

**Fork Timing Drill**

- Attacker presents Threat A from mount (armbar setup)
- Defender responds with the predicted defense (turning to the side)
- Attacker must transition to Threat B (back take) within 2 seconds of the defense arriving
- If the transition is too slow, reset. Builds the speed of recognizing and exploiting the fork

**Dilemma Sparring**

- 5-minute rounds with a rule: the attacker must use at least one dilemma per attack sequence
- After each sequence (successful or failed), both partners identify: what was Threat A? What was Threat B? Did the attacker read the defense correctly?
- Develops the habit of thinking in forks, not in single attacks

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on recognizing that positions contain natural dilemmas and on deliberately constructing two-threat sequences.

At higher levels, dilemmas become invisible to the opponent. The advanced player's attacks flow so naturally from one to the next that the opponent does not realize they are in a fork until they have already chosen the losing path. Multiple dilemmas layer on top of each other - the defense to one dilemma leads directly into the next. The opponent experiences not a series of choices but a continuous narrowing of options until the only remaining option is the tap.
