# Chaining Attacks

---

## Definition

Chaining attacks means linking multiple techniques into a continuous sequence where each technique flows into the next without resetting to a neutral position. A chain is not a list of separate attacks performed one after another - it is a connected series where the end of one attack is the beginning of the next. The opponent's defense to attack one creates the entry for attack two. The defense to attack two creates the entry for attack three. The chain continues until the opponent runs out of defenses.

---

## Why It Matters

Dilemma creation (Concept 1) presents the opponent with two threats at a fork. Chaining attacks extends that principle across time - it creates a sequence of forks where each defense leads to the next threat, and the threats keep coming until the finish arrives.

Without chaining:

- Each attack is an isolated event with a full reset between attempts
- The opponent defends one attack, recovers to a neutral position, and waits for the next - they are never under continuous pressure
- The attacker must rebuild position, re-isolate, and re-expose for every attempt - each attack costs full setup time
- Failed attacks result in lost energy with nothing to show for it - the work is wasted
- As opponents improve, isolated attacks become increasingly unlikely to succeed on the first attempt

With chaining:

- The opponent is under continuous threat - there is no recovery window between attacks
- Each defense degrades the opponent's position slightly, making subsequent attacks easier
- The attacker's work is never wasted - a failed attack flows into the next attack, preserving the energy invested in isolation and exposure
- Finishing rates increase dramatically because the opponent is defending the third or fourth attack in a chain, not the first
- Chains are demoralizing - the opponent feels the pressure building with each link and knows the finish is approaching

This is the concept that converts individual submission knowledge (Level 6) into an offensive system. A single submission is a tool. A chain of submissions is a machine.

---

## Core Idea

Never reset after a failed attack. The failed attack is the setup for the next one. Keep the chain going until the finish arrives.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**How chains work mechanically.** A chain operates on a simple principle: every defense to an attack creates a specific body position. That body position is vulnerable to a different attack. The attacker does not fight the defense - they flow around it to the next link.

Example chain from mount:

1. **Armbar attempt** → Opponent turns to their side to protect the arm
2. **Back take from the turn** → Opponent defends the hooks and turns back to face the attacker
3. **Mounted position recovery** → Opponent is now flat on their back with arms extended from the scramble
4. **Arm triangle from the flattened position** → Opponent frames and bridges to escape
5. **Mount re-establishment from the bridge** → Return to step 1 with fresh attack options

Each step is caused by the opponent's defense to the previous step. The attacker does not create these transitions from nothing - the opponent's defense creates them. The attacker's skill is recognizing and exploiting each transition as it appears.

**The three elements of a chain link.**

Every link in a chain has:

1. **The attack**: The technique being executed
2. **The expected defense**: What the opponent will most likely do to stop it
3. **The transition**: How the defense flows into the next attack

These are the same three components as a dilemma (Concept 1), but applied sequentially. A dilemma is a single fork. A chain is a series of forks connected in sequence.

**Common chain patterns.**

_The submission loop._ A set of 2-3 submissions that cycle back to each other:

- Triangle → opponent pulls arm out → armbar → opponent stacks and turns → triangle re-entry → repeat
- Kimura → opponent straightens arm to defend → armbar → opponent bends arm and turns → kimura re-entry → repeat
- Rear naked choke → opponent defends neck with hands → armbar on the defending arm → opponent pulls arm back to body → choke re-entry → repeat

These loops have no end - the opponent circles between the same two or three threats until one finishes. Each cycle degrades the defense slightly as fatigue builds.

_The positional escalation chain._ Attacks that climb the positional hierarchy:

- Side control kimura attempt → opponent defends → mount transition → armbar from mount → opponent defends → back take → rear naked choke

Each failed attack advances the position. The opponent is defending submissions while simultaneously losing positional ground. By the time the third or fourth attack arrives, the attacker is in a superior position with a weakened defense in front of them.

_The guard attack chain._ Submissions from guard that flow into sweeps and back:

- Triangle attempt → opponent postures to escape → hip bump using the posture → arrive in mount → attack from mount → if mount is lost, return to guard → new triangle entry

The chain crosses between guard and top position, with each transition creating new attack opportunities.

**Building natural chains.** Chains are not memorized sequences - they are built from understanding which attacks flow into which defenses flow into which attacks. The building blocks:

- **What does the armbar defense create?** The opponent turns, extends the far arm, or bridges - each of these creates a different next link
- **What does the triangle defense create?** The opponent pulls the arm out (armbar), postures up (hip bump sweep), or stacks (stand-up escape or angle adjustment)
- **What does the choke defense create?** The opponent's hands go to the neck (arms exposed for locks), the opponent turns (position change), or the opponent postures (guard opens)

By knowing what each defense creates, the attacker can build chains on the fly - they do not need to memorize a fixed sequence. They need to understand the transitions between attacks.

**The chain does not require speed.** Chains are not about moving fast. They are about moving continuously. The opponent gets no rest, no reset, no moment to gather themselves and plan a defense from scratch. The pace can be deliberate - each link executed with full control (Level 6, Concept 4) - as long as the transitions happen without gaps.

Gaps kill chains. A pause between attacks allows the opponent to reset, recover grips, rebuild defensive structures, and defend the next attack as an isolated event rather than as part of a degrading sequence. The chain's power is its continuity.

---

## Key Principles

- Never reset after a failed attack - the defense is the setup for the next attack
- Every defense creates a body position that is vulnerable to a different technique
- Chains are not memorized sequences - they are built from understanding what each defense opens
- The chain's power is continuity - no gaps, no resets, no recovery moments for the opponent
- Each link in the chain degrades the opponent's defense - the first attack is hardest to land, the fourth is easiest
- Common chains form natural loops: triangle-armbar, kimura-armbar, choke-armbar
- Positional escalation chains advance position while attacking - failed attacks still earn positional improvement

---

## Common Mistakes

- Resetting to neutral after each failed attack instead of flowing to the next link
- Committing so fully to one attack that the transition to the next is impossible - overcommitment kills the chain
- Not recognizing what the defense opens - the transition is available but the attacker does not see it
- Moving fast but not moving continuously - speed without connection between attacks is just random scrambling
- Memorizing fixed chains without understanding the underlying transitions - when the opponent does something unexpected, the fixed chain breaks
- Allowing gaps between links - the pause lets the opponent reset and the chain's compounding pressure is lost

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Mount Attack Chain (Submission Loop)

- **Link 1**: Threaten the armbar - isolate the near arm, begin setting up the pivot
- **Defense**: Opponent turns to their side, tucking the arm
- **Link 2**: The turn exposes the back - slide the arm under for a seatbelt, begin inserting hooks
- **Defense**: Opponent turns back to face the attacker, fighting the hooks
- **Link 3**: Opponent is now flat with arms extended from the scramble - cross collar choke or arm triangle
- **Defense**: Opponent frames to prevent the choke, extending the arm
- **Link 4**: Return to an armbar on the framing arm - we are back at Link 1 with a more fatigued opponent
- The loop continues: armbar → back → choke → armbar → back → choke

### Guard Attack Chain (Triangle-Armbar Loop)

- **Link 1**: From closed guard with broken posture, throw legs up for a triangle
- **Defense**: Opponent pulls the trapped arm out (standard triangle defense)
- **Link 2**: The pulling arm is extended - catch it and pivot hips for an armbar
- **Defense**: Opponent stacks forward to relieve the armbar pressure, bending the arm
- **Link 3**: The stack puts the opponent back in closed guard range with broken posture - re-enter the triangle
- **Defense**: Opponent pulls the arm out again
- **Link 4**: Armbar again - each cycle the defense is weaker because the opponent is more fatigued and their posture is more broken
- The loop continues until either the triangle or the armbar finishes

### Back Control Chain (Choke to Armbar)

- **Link 1**: From back control, begin the rear naked choke - arm under the chin
- **Defense**: Opponent grabs the choking wrist with both hands, pulling it down
- **Link 2**: Both hands are committed to the choking arm - the defending arms are exposed. Peel one hand and transition to an armbar on the defending arm
- **Defense**: Opponent pulls the arm back to defend the armbar, turning toward the attacker
- **Link 3**: The turn movement - hooks are still in. The opponent is turning into the attacker, which re-exposes the neck. Re-enter the choke
- **Defense**: Opponent grabs the choking arm again
- **Link 4**: Back to Link 2 - armbar on the defending arm
- The loop continues: choke → armbar → choke → armbar

### Passing Chain (Positional Escalation)

- **Link 1**: Threaten a torreando pass to the left
- **Defense**: Opponent turns hips and frames to block the left side
- **Link 2**: The hip turn opens the right side - leg drag to the right
- **Defense**: Opponent hip escapes to prevent the leg drag from consolidating
- **Link 3**: The hip escape creates space - but the opponent's back is partially exposed. Take the back from the hip escape
- **Defense**: Opponent flattens and turns to prevent the back take
- **Link 4**: The flattening gives side control - consolidate and begin the submission chain from side control
- Each failed pass advanced the position: open guard → half-passed → back exposure → side control

---

## Simple Mental Model

A combination lock. Each number you enter brings you closer to opening it. If you stop halfway, the lock resets and you start over. But if you keep entering numbers - each one flowing from the last - the lock opens. Each attack is a number. Each defense tells you the next number. Keep entering them without stopping and the lock opens.

## Optional

### Drills

**Two-Attack Chain Drill**

- From mount, execute a two-link chain: armbar attempt → back take on the defense
- Repeat 10 times at 50% resistance. The defender always makes the predicted defense (turning to the side)
- Then switch: back take attempt → opponent turns back → armbar on the return
- Builds the smallest unit of chaining - one attack flowing into one counter

**Loop Drill**

- From closed guard, execute the triangle-armbar loop
- Triangle → opponent pulls arm → armbar → opponent stacks → triangle → opponent pulls arm → armbar
- Continue for 3 minutes without resetting to neutral
- Partner provides 50% resistance and makes the predicted defenses
- Builds the habit of continuous attacking without gaps

**No-Reset Sparring**

- 5-minute rounds where the attacker has one rule: never stop attacking
- If an attack fails, immediately transition to the next attack - no returning to a holding position
- After the round, count how many consecutive attacks were in the longest chain
- Builds the habit of flowing between attacks rather than resetting

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on learning the common chains (triangle-armbar, choke-armbar, kimura-armbar) and on building the habit of transitioning between attacks without resetting.

At higher levels, chains become improvised rather than memorized. The advanced player does not run a fixed sequence - they read each defense in real time and flow to whatever the defense opens. Chains branch into sub-chains. A single starting attack might lead to five different second links depending on the defense, and each of those second links might lead to three different third links. The chain becomes a tree, and the attacker navigates the tree based on the opponent's choices. At the highest level, the opponent does not experience a chain - they experience an overwhelming, continuous offensive pressure where every movement they make seems to make things worse.
