# Troubleshooting Guide

---

Common problems mapped to specific concepts and levels. When something consistently goes wrong in training, find the symptom below and follow the prescription back to the concept that will fix it.

---

## Survival and Defense Problems

### "I panic when I'm in a bad position"

**Root cause**: Lack of Level 1 positional awareness — the student does not recognize where they are on the hierarchy, so the body defaults to a fear response.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 1, Concept 1 (Positional Hierarchy)
- Drill: Positional Awareness Drill (Drill 3) — practice identifying positions without physical escape
- Drill: Structure Retention Drill (Drill 4) — hold structure for 60 seconds under pressure to build comfort

---

### "I get submitted before I can even start escaping"

**Root cause**: Skipping "survive before escape" — the student jumps to escape without first protecting the neck and limbs.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 2, Concept 1 (Survival Before Escape)
- Revisit the Level 2 escape sequence — Step 1 (Survive) must happen before Step 2 (Align)
- Drill: Constraint Drill (Drill 6) — enforced sequence, no escape attempt until survival is secured

---

### "I can escape sometimes but I keep ending up back in the same bad position"

**Root cause**: Incomplete escape — the student creates space but does not recover to guard (Step 6 of the escape sequence).

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 2, Concept 5 (Guard Recovery Logic)
- Focus on the elbow-knee connection — the escape is not complete until guard is established
- Drill: Escape-to-Guard Sparring (Drill 9) — only a successful escape that ends in guard counts

---

### "I exhaust myself trying to escape"

**Root cause**: Muscling the escape instead of using frames and structure.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Core Mechanical Concepts — Frames section
- Re-read Level 2, Concept 2 (Frame Creation and Maintenance) — frames use bone, not muscle
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 5 (Energy Efficiency) — structure first, muscles last
- Drill: Structure Retention Drill (Drill 4) — practice holding structure without escape attempts

---

## Control Problems

### "I can get to side control but I can't hold it"

**Root cause**: Missing one or more of the five Level 3 concepts — usually weight distribution or failure to prevent movement before it starts.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 3 integration section — all five concepts must be applied simultaneously
- Check specifically: Is the crossface driving the head? Are the hips controlled? Is weight low and distributed?
- Drill: The Complete Pin (Drill 10) — 5-minute rounds, control only, diagnose which concept fails first
- Drill: Diagnostic Drill (Drill 12) — after each escape, identify the specific concept that broke down

---

### "I keep getting reversed from mount"

**Root cause**: Usually poor base management — the student reaches for attacks and loses weight distribution.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 1, Concept 2 (Base and Posture)
- Re-read Level 3, Concept 3 (Weight Distribution) — hips must stay heavy during attacks
- Practice mounting with grapevines and hands posted wide — no submission attempts until mount is stable for 60 seconds

---

### "I get the position but lose it when I try to submit"

**Root cause**: Attacking before control is established — violating the principle of "control before submission."

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 3, Concept 5 (Preventing Movement Before Attacking)
- Re-read Level 6, Concept 4 (Control During Submission) — the position must survive the submission attempt
- Drill: The Complete Pin (Drill 10) — practice control without submissions until control is effortless

---

## Guard Problems

### "My guard gets passed immediately"

**Root cause**: Usually missing distance management or inside leg position — the opponent is already past the first two defensive layers before the guard player reacts.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 4, Concept 1 (Distance Management)
- Re-read Level 4, Concept 3 (Inside Leg Position) — legs must be inside before the passer commits
- Drill: Connection Maintenance Under Pressure (Drill 16) — maintain at least two connections at all times
- Drill: Guard Reset Drill (Drill 14) — practice recovering from nearly passed

---

### "I can hold guard but I never attack"

**Root cause**: Missing off-balancing — the guard player maintains position but never disrupts the opponent's balance enough to create openings.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 4, Concept 2 (Off-Balancing)
- Re-read Level 4, Concept 6 (Sweep vs Submission Decision-Making) — the guard player must read the opponent's weight and choose
- Drill: Sweep-Submit Alternation (Drill 15) — force one attack per exchange

---

### "I can't retain guard against good passers"

**Root cause**: Over-reliance on a single retention layer instead of using the full layer system.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 4, Concept 5 (Retention Layers) — feet → knees → hips → frames, and the ability to climb back up
- Drill: Guard Reset Drill (Drill 14) — start from Layer 3 (nearly passed), climb back to Layer 1

---

## Passing Problems

### "I can't get past the legs"

**Root cause**: Trying to pass without establishing control first, or fighting the legs instead of systematically working through the gates.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 5, Concept 1 (Control Before Passing) — establish grips and base before attempting the pass
- Re-read Level 5, Concept 2 (Gate Sequence) — beat feet first, then knees, then hips
- Drill: Gate Identification Drill (Drill 18) — at "freeze," identify which gate is blocking and what to do next

---

### "I pass the guard but they re-guard immediately"

**Root cause**: Not consolidating after the pass — the passer arrives at side control but does not immediately establish crossface and hip control.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 5, Concept 4 (Clear Frames Before Advancing) — frames must be cleared during the pass, not after
- Re-read Level 3 (all concepts) — control must be established immediately upon arrival
- Drill: Re-Guard Prevention (Drill 21) — hold after pass for 10 seconds, re-pass if guard is recovered

---

### "I can only do one type of pass"

**Root cause**: Lack of approach flexibility — the student only knows pressure passing or only knows movement passing.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 5, Concept 5 (Pressure vs Movement Passing)
- Drill: Pressure-to-Movement Switching (Drill 19) — switch approaches on command

---

## Submission Problems

### "I can set up submissions but I can't finish them"

**Root cause**: Usually skipping the "break defense" phase — the submission setup is good but the opponent's defensive grip or frame is still intact.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 6, Concept 3 (Breaking Defensive Structures)
- Re-read Level 6, Concept 5 (Mechanical Efficiency) — are you finishing with hips or with hands?
- Drill: Efficiency Comparison (Drill 24) — feel the difference between muscling and mechanics
- Drill: Submission Diagnostic (Drill 23) — freeze and identify which phase failed

---

### "I lose position when I go for submissions"

**Root cause**: Launching submissions without sufficient positional security — violating "position before submission."

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 6, Concept 1 (Isolation Before Attack) — the target must be isolated before committing to the attack
- Re-read Level 6, Concept 4 (Control During Submission) — the position must be maintained throughout
- Drill: Five-Phase Submission Drill (Drill 22) — complete all five phases in order, do not skip to the finish

---

### "I try the same submission every time"

**Root cause**: Missing the concept of attack selection and chaining — the student has one move instead of a system.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 4, Concept 6 (Sweep vs Submission Decision-Making) — offense is reactive to what the opponent gives
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 1 (Dilemma Creation) — present two threats at once
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 2 (Chaining Attacks) — when one is defended, flow to the next
- Drill: Strangle and Lock Circuit (Drill 25) — cycle through four submissions
- Drill: Dilemma Chain Drill (Drill 28) — chain 3+ dilemmas in sequence

---

## System-Level Problems

### "I feel like I know techniques but I can't put it all together"

**Root cause**: Missing Level 7 — the student has components but not a system.

**Fix**:

- Read Level 7 in its entirety — all six concepts and the integration section
- Focus on the Decision-Making Checklist in the Level 7 integration
- Drill: Full System Rounds (Drill 27) — 8-minute rounds with post-round debrief
- Drill: Complete Offensive System Drill (Drill 32) — practice the full pass → control → submit chain

---

### "I run out of energy before the match is over"

**Root cause**: Poor energy management — the student either never rests or always rests, instead of cycling between bursts and recovery holds.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 7, Concept 5 (Energy Efficiency) — structure holds, muscles rest
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 6 (Strategic Pacing) — burst when opportunity is high, recover when it is low
- Drill: Energy Budget Drill (Drill 31) — self-rate energy at 2-minute intervals and analyze the curve
- Identify energy-negative habits: death grips, unnecessary pressure, constant movement without purpose

---

### "I can't impose my game on my opponent"

**Root cause**: Reactive instead of proactive — the student defends and responds but does not dictate the exchange.

**Fix**:

- Re-read Level 7, Concept 1 (Dilemma Creation) — force the opponent to choose, not you
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 3 (Anticipating Reactions) — know what they will do before they do it
- Re-read Level 7, Concept 6 (Strategic Pacing) — impose your tempo
- Drill: Pace Control Drill (Drill 30) — declare your pacing strategy and assess whether you controlled it
- Drill: Dilemma Chain Drill (Drill 28) — practice continuous offensive pressure

---

### "I do well in drilling but not in live rolling"

**Root cause**: The gap between isolated drilling and live application. The student understands the concepts in a controlled environment but cannot access them under pressure.

**Fix**:

- Increase positional sparring time — the bridge between drilling and rolling
- Use the Training Plan's session structure: concept → drill → positional sparring → open rolling
- Focus on one to two concepts per rolling session — do not try to apply everything at once
- Post-round debrief: after each roll, identify one moment where a concept applied and one where it was missed
