# Positional Reference Guide

---

Every major position in jiu jitsu is broken down using the same eight-point template defined in the Guideline Framework. This guide is a quick-reference companion to the main curriculum: use it to look up any position and immediately understand its goals, controls, threats, and connections.

---

## Mount - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

Maintain the mount position while setting up submissions. Mount is one of the two apex positions: the goal is to stay here and finish.

### 2. Key Controls

- Hips heavy on the opponent's torso: weight drives through the pelvis, not the knees
- Knees squeezed against the opponent's ribs or tucked under the armpits (high mount)
- Grapevines or hooks on the legs to prevent bridging
- Hands posted wide for base or controlling the opponent's arms

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent bridges (upa) to reverse: especially dangerous when the top player is reaching for attacks
- Opponent elbow-knee escapes to half guard or guard by creating space on one side
- Opponent traps an arm during a submission attempt and uses it as a reversal lever

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Bridge to create space and reverse the position
- Elbow-knee escape to recover guard
- Survive and wait for a mistake to exploit

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain base: do not get rolled during submission attempts
- Keep hips low: elevation creates bridging space
- Control at least one arm or the head at all times: if both arms are free, the opponent has escape tools

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Armbar (isolate an arm, swing the leg over the head)
- Cross-collar choke or ezekiel choke (direct neck attacks)
- Arm triangle (head and arm, transition to side mount to finish)
- Americana or wrist lock (arm attacks from low mount)
- Transition to back control if the opponent turns to escape

### 7. Transitions

- To back control: when the opponent turns to escape, slide hooks in
- To side control: when mount is unstable, drop to side control to regroup
- To submission phase: when an arm or neck is available, enter the five-phase submission sequence

### 8. Submissions

- Armbar, cross-collar choke, ezekiel, arm triangle, americana, mounted triangle

## Mount - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Escape the mount and recover to guard. This is a Tier 1 defensive position: survival first, escape second.

### 2. Key Controls

- Elbows tight to the body: do not allow arm isolation
- Hands protecting the neck: chin tucked, frames on the biceps or chest
- Feet flat on the mat: ready to bridge

### 3. Primary Threats

- Chokes: cross-collar, ezekiel, arm triangle
- Arm attacks: armbar (high mount), americana (low mount)
- The opponent climbing to high mount: each inch higher reduces escape options

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Isolate an arm or expose the neck
- Flatten the bottom player and climb to high mount
- Force a reaction that opens a submission

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Protect the neck at all times: tuck the chin, frame the chest
- Keep elbows connected to the body: isolated arms get attacked
- Do not panic bridge: time the bridge to the opponent's weight shift

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Very limited: this is a survival position
- Trap-and-roll (upa) reversal when the opponent overcommits weight
- Elbow-knee escape to half guard or guard

### 7. Transitions

- To half guard: successful elbow-knee escape recovers half guard
- To guard: full elbow-knee sequence or upa to closed guard
- To top position: successful trap-and-roll reversal lands in the opponent's guard

### 8. Submissions

- None from standard mount bottom: escape first, attack later

## Knee-on-Belly - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

Maintain knee pressure on the opponent's torso while using superior mobility to attack or transition. Knee-on-belly is a high-mobility dominant position that prioritizes movement speed and transition access over grinding stability.

### 2. Key Controls

- Knee driven into the opponent's solar plexus or lower ribs: active downward pressure through the shin
- Far-side leg posted wide for base and balance
- Near-side hand gripping the opponent's collar, lapel, or far-side arm
- Far-side hand controlling the near-side hip or belt to prevent turning

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent turns into you (the near-side turn: most common escape attempt)
- Opponent pushes the knee off with hands
- Opponent hip bumps and turns away, creating distance to recover guard
- Opponent catches half guard by trapping the posted leg

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Push the knee off and recover guard or half guard
- Turn into the top player to enter single-leg or come to knees
- Scoot hips away to create enough distance to reinsert legs

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Keep the knee heavy and centered: a light knee invites push-offs
- Maintain wide base with the posted leg: do not let both knees come together
- React to the near-side turn immediately: if they turn in, transition to mount or re-spin to the far side
- Do not chase: if the position is lost, settle into side control rather than losing everything

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Baseball bat choke (gi: collar grips set before dropping)
- Far-side armbar (spin to armbar when opponent pushes the knee)
- Near-side collar choke (cross-collar grip, drop weight)
- Transition to mount (step over when opponent is flat and not pushing)
- Transition to back take (when opponent turns away)

### 7. Transitions

- To mount: step the posted leg over the opponent's body when they stop pushing
- To side control: drop knee and settle hips when pressure is lost or position feels unstable
- To armbar: spin to armbar when opponent pushes up on the knee with near-side arm
- To back control: when opponent turns away, release knee, insert hooks

### 8. Submissions

- Baseball bat choke, cross-collar choke, far-side armbar, near-side collar choke
- Many submissions from knee-on-belly come from the opponent's escape reactions: their attempts to push off or turn away expose limbs and the neck

## Knee-on-Belly - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Escape the pressure and recover to guard or half guard. Knee-on-belly bottom requires immediate action: the pressure compels movement, which the top player exploits.

### 2. Key Controls

- Frame against the knee with both hands to prevent it from sinking deeper
- Protect the collar: cross-collar chokes are the primary threat
- Keep elbows tight: extended arms are easy submission targets

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent transitions to mount if you stop defending
- Opponent drops to armbar if you push the knee with a straight arm
- Opponent attacks collar chokes while you are focused on the knee

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Apply crushing pressure to force a reaction
- Use your defensive reactions to set up submissions
- Transition to mount or back control if you freeze

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Do not push the knee with a straight arm: this invites armbars
- Frame with both hands: one on the knee, one on the hip
- Turn to the near side (toward the opponent) to create space for guard recovery
- Act quickly: knee-on-belly is designed to punish inaction

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Very limited from bottom: escape first
- If opponent is sloppy with base, a strong hip bump and turn-in can create a single-leg or scramble opportunity

### 7. Transitions

- To half guard: block the knee and hip escape to catch the posted leg
- To guard: push the knee off and insert both legs, reestablishing a guard framework
- To turtle (turn away and come to knees (less ideal) exposes back momentarily)

### 8. Submissions

- None from standard knee-on-belly bottom: escape first, attack later

## Side Control - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

Maintain the pin while preparing submissions or transitions to mount or back. Side control is the most common dominant position and the primary "base camp" for top-player offense.

### 2. Key Controls

- Crossface: shoulder and head pressure driving the opponent's face away from the top player
- Underhook: arm under the opponent's far arm, controlling the far shoulder
- Hip pressure: chest and hip weight distributed across the opponent's torso
- Knee-to-hip wedge: near knee blocks the opponent's hip to prevent shrimping

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent frames and shrimps to recover guard
- Opponent gets an underhook and comes to knees (turtle)
- Opponent bridges and creates enough space to insert a knee

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Create frames to build space
- Win the underhook to change the dynamic
- Shrimp to reinsert the knee and recover half guard or guard

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain crossface: if the bottom player faces the top player, escape percentage increases dramatically
- Block the near hip: if the hip is free, shrimping is easy
- Stay heavy: if the weight lifts, frames become effective

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Kimura: isolate the near arm below the top player's body
- Americana: isolate the far arm using the crossface side
- Arm triangle: opponent defends neck with the far arm, creating the head-and-arm triangle
- North-south choke: slide to north-south for direct neck access
- Transition to mount for broader submission options

### 7. Transitions

- To mount: slide the knee across the belly as the opponent's frames weaken
- To back: when the opponent turns to escape, slide hooks in behind
- To north-south: when the opponent is well-framed against side control, change angle
- To knee-on-belly: when dynamic pressure is needed or the opponent is turtling

### 8. Submissions

- Kimura, americana, arm triangle, north-south choke, paper cutter choke, baseball bat choke

## Side Control - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Escape side control and recover to guard. A Tier 1 defensive position.

### 2. Key Controls

- Near-side frame: forearm across the opponent's neck or bicep to create space
- Far-side frame: hand on the opponent's far hip or far shoulder to prevent weight transfer
- Bridge structure: feet positioned to bridge toward the opponent and create space

### 3. Primary Threats

- Kimura and americana if arms are displaced
- Cross-body arm attacks if far arm is reached
- Opponent transitions to mount or north-south
- Being flattened: progressive weight breaks down all frame structure over time

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Maintain the pin and exhaust the bottom player
- Isolate an arm for submission
- Advance to mount or back control

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain frames constantly: the moment frames drop, weight crushes
- Do not allow the crossface to drive the head flat: fight for head position
- Keep elbows tight: do not allow arm isolation

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Very limited: frame, create space, escape
- If the opponent overreaches for a submission, the underhook becomes available
- Ghost escape when the opponent's weight is poorly distributed

### 7. Transitions

- To guard: shrimp to create space, insert the knee, recover full guard or half guard
- To turtle (if the opponent allows space above, turn to knees (temporary) recover guard from turtle)
- To standing: frame to create space, technical stand-up

### 8. Submissions

- Rare from this position: occasional kimura or guillotine if the opponent's head drops low

## Back Control - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

The ultimate dominant position. Maintain back control and finish with a strangle. This is the end of the positional hierarchy before submission.

### 2. Key Controls

- Seatbelt grip: over-under control of the torso (one arm over the shoulder, one arm under the armpit)
- Hooks: both feet hooked inside the opponent's thighs, controlling hip movement
- Body triangle (optional): legs locked around the torso for maximum hip control
- Chin-strap or collar grip: direct control of the head to set up chokes

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent peels the top hook and slides hips to the mat
- Opponent fights the choking hand and creates space to turn
- Opponent drops to the side of the underhook to escape

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Clear the hooks and get hips to the mat
- Fight the hands and prevent the choke from locking
- Turn to face the attacker and recover guard

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain hooks or body triangle: if hooks are cleared, the position is lost
- Maintain seatbelt: if the grip breaks, the opponent can turn and escape
- Follow the opponent's movement: if they scoot, follow; if they turn, re-adjust

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Rear naked choke: the highest-percentage submission in the sport
- Collar chokes: cross-collar, bow-and-arrow
- Arm attacks: armbar when the opponent extends an arm to defend the neck
- Triangle from the back: when arm positioning creates the opportunity

### 7. Transitions

- To mount: if hooks are lost or the opponent escapes to a neutral position, transition to mount before losing all control
- To turtle top: if the opponent escapes hooks but is still turned away, maintain top turtle control
- Back to side control: if the back is fully lost, secure side control as recovery

### 8. Submissions

- Rear naked choke, bow-and-arrow choke, short choke, armbar from back, triangle from back

## Back Control - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Survive and escape. The most dangerous position in jiu jitsu. Priority zero: protect the neck.

### 2. Key Controls

- Two-hand grip defense (both hands fighting the choking arm) one on the wrist, one on the elbow
- Chin tuck: chin pressed to the chest to deny access to the neck
- Shoulder shrug: shoulder elevated on the choking side to close the neck space

### 3. Primary Threats

- Rear naked choke: the primary and most dangerous threat
- Collar chokes from behind
- Arm attacks if the bottom player overcommits one arm to defense

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Lock the rear naked choke
- Strip hand defenses to access the neck
- Maintain hooks and prevent the escape

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Protect the neck at all costs: this is the number one priority, above everything
- Fight the hands: clear the grip before any escape attempt
- Do not extend the arms: a reaching arm is an isolated arm

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- None: this is purely a defensive position. Escape first.

### 7. Transitions

- Escape to guard: fight grips, clear the top hook, drop hips to the mat, turn to face, recover guard
- Escape to turtle (worst case mid-escape position) immediately recover to guard or standing from turtle

### 8. Submissions

- None from this position

## Closed Guard - Top (In Opponent's Guard)

### 1. Positional Goal

Maintain posture, open the guard, and pass. Being inside someone's closed guard is a slight disadvantage: the guard player has more offensive options than the top player.

### 2. Key Controls

- Posture (head up, spine straight, hands on the hips or lapels) do not let posture break
- Hip distance: maintain space between the torso and the guard player's hips
- Grip control: strip threatening grips before they establish

### 3. Primary Threats

- Guard player breaks posture and attacks submissions (triangle, armbar, omoplata)
- Guard player sweeps (hip bump, scissor, flower)
- Guard player climbs to high guard or rubber guard, trapping the top player's posture

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Break posture to create submission and sweep opportunities
- Use legs and hips to control the top player's movement
- Off-balance the top player to set up attacks

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain posture at all times: posture is the primary defense against everything from closed guard
- Keep elbows inside: arms extended or reaching create armbar and triangle openings
- Keep base: do not get swept by ignoring weight distribution

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Guard opening sequences: posture up, control hips, open the legs
- Very limited submission options from inside closed guard: the priority is to pass

### 7. Transitions

- To open guard (standing/kneeling): open the closed guard and begin the guard pass
- To half guard: if one leg is cleared, arrive in top half guard and continue passing
- To side control: complete the pass

### 8. Submissions

- Limited from this position: occasional can-opener setup, ezekiel, or guillotine if the opponent sits up

## Closed Guard - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Control the opponent's posture and attack with sweeps and submissions. Closed guard is the strongest offensive bottom position.

### 2. Key Controls

- Leg lock around the waist: closed ankles controlling hip distance
- Posture control: collar grip, overhook, or head control pulling the opponent down
- Angle: hips offset to one side to create attack angles

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent postures up and opens the guard: leading to guard passing
- Opponent stacks during submission attempts
- Opponent stands to begin a standing guard pass

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Maintain posture to prevent submission setups
- Open the guard to begin passing
- Neutralize off-balancing attempts through base

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Do not let the guard be opened without being in a strong retention position
- Do not overcommit to sweeps that leave the back exposed
- Do not hang on submissions that are clearly defended: transition instead

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Sweeps: hip bump, scissor sweep, flower sweep, pendulum
- Submissions: triangle, armbar, omoplata, guillotine, kimura
- The sweep-vs-submission decision framework (Level 4): read the opponent's posture and weight distribution to choose the correct attack

### 7. Transitions

- To open guard: if the opponent stands or opens the guard, transition to an open guard system
- To mount: successful sweep lands in mount
- To top position: successful sweep lands in the opponent's guard (top)

### 8. Submissions

- Triangle, armbar, omoplata, guillotine, kimura, cross-collar choke, gogoplata

## Half Guard - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

Complete the pass to side control or mount. Top half guard is an offensive position: the pass is in progress.

### 2. Key Controls

- Crossface: shoulder pressure driving the opponent's head away
- Underhook control: winning the underhook on the far side to deny the opponent's sweep
- Weight distribution: heavy hips to prevent the opponent from creating angles
- Knee positioning: working to free the trapped leg

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent wins the underhook and comes to a sweep position
- Opponent inserts a knee shield creating distance and leverage
- Opponent off-balances the top player and reverses

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Win the underhook
- Insert the knee shield
- Sweep or recover to full guard

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain crossface: do not concede head position
- Deny the underhook: whizzer (overhook) defense if the opponent gets it
- Keep weight low: do not create space for the knee shield insertion

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Complete the knee cut pass
- Switch to leg weave or staple pass
- Transition to mount by stepping over

### 7. Transitions

- To side control: complete the pass
- To mount: step over after flattening the opponent
- To back: if the opponent turns away during the pass, take the back

### 8. Submissions

- Arm triangle, darce choke, anaconda, kimura during pass transitions

## Half Guard - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Sweep the opponent or recover full guard. Half guard bottom is a Tier 2 recovery position that has significant offensive potential.

### 2. Key Controls

- Knee shield: shin across the opponent's chest or belly creating distance and leverage
- Underhook: arm under the opponent's far armpit, controlling the far shoulder
- Bottom hook: inside hook on the opponent's trapped leg to prevent posture

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent crossfaces and flattens: killing the underhook and guard retention options
- Opponent frees the trapped leg and completes the pass to side control
- Opponent uses heavy hip pressure to immobilize the bottom player

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Flatten the bottom player
- Free the trapped leg and pass
- Control the head and deny the underhook

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Do not get flattened: maintain a frame or knee shield at all times
- Do not lose the underhook: it is the primary offensive tool from half guard bottom
- Do not allow both shoulders to touch the mat simultaneously

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Sweeps: old school sweep (underhook to the back), dog fight, electric chair
- Back take: from the dog fight position (both players on knees with underhooks)
- Guard recovery: insert the free knee to recover closed or butterfly guard

### 7. Transitions

- To full guard: insert the knee and recover guard
- To dog fight: come to knees with the underhook
- To back control: take the back from the dog fight
- To top position: sweep to land in side control or mount

### 8. Submissions

- Kimura, guillotine, loop choke (during sweep transitions)

## Turtle - Top

### 1. Positional Goal

Take the back or return the opponent to a controlled bottom position (side control). Turtle top is a transitional offensive position.

### 2. Key Controls

- Seatbelt grip or waist control: maintaining connection to the turtled opponent
- Chest-to-back pressure: driving weight forward to flatten the opponent
- Hook threats: threatening to insert hooks for back control

### 3. Primary Threats

- Opponent sits through or granby rolls to recover guard
- Opponent stands up and disengages
- Opponent reaches through and takes a single leg

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Recover guard by sitting through
- Stand up and face the attacker
- Create space to disengage

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Maintain chest-to-back contact: if the opponent creates space, they escape
- Do not overcommit to one side: the opponent will roll to the other
- Follow the opponent's movement: stay connected

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Back take: insert hooks from the seatbelt
- Clock choke: from a north-south angle on the turtle
- Turnovers: cradle or roll the opponent to their back for side control

### 7. Transitions

- To back control: insert hooks, establish seatbelt
- To side control: turnover to a pin
- To front headlock: if the opponent's head is available, snap down and control

### 8. Submissions

- Clock choke, crucifix position attacks, darce choke, anaconda choke

## Turtle - Bottom

### 1. Positional Goal

Recover guard or stand up. Turtle is a Tier 2 transitional position: do not stay here longer than necessary.

### 2. Key Controls

- Elbows tight to the body: prevent the opponent from reaching through
- Chin tucked: protect the neck from chokes
- Hips ready to sit through or stand

### 3. Primary Threats

- Back take: the opponent inserts hooks and takes the back (Tier 1)
- Clock choke: the opponent uses the gi or arm to choke from the side
- Turnovers: the opponent rolls or cradles to put the bottom player on their back

### 4. Opponent Objectives

- Take the back
- Choke from the turtle position
- Turn the bottom player over for a pin

### 5. Defensive Responsibilities

- Do not allow hooks: keep elbows against the thighs to block hook insertion
- Do not allow seatbelt: keep arms tight and fight any grip that reaches across the chest
- Move immediately (turtle is temporary) every second spent here increases the opponent's back-take percentage

### 6. Offensive Opportunities

- Very limited: the priority is to escape
- Sit-through to guard: roll to one hip and recover guard
- Granby roll: rolling inversion to arrive in guard
- Stand up: post on the hands and stand to a wrestling stance

### 7. Transitions

- To guard: sit-through, granby roll, or Peterson roll to arrive in guard
- To standing: stand up and face the opponent
- If the opponent takes the back: transition to back control bottom (Tier 1 defense)

### 8. Submissions

- None: escape first

## How to Use This Guide

Each entry follows the same eight-point template. This consistency means that once you learn how to read one position, you can read any position in seconds.

Use this guide:

- **Before training**: Look up the positions you will be working and review the key controls, threats, and transitions
- **After training**: When a position went wrong, look it up here and compare what happened with what should happen: identify which element broke down
- **During curriculum study**: Cross-reference the concept sections with these positional breakdowns to see how concepts map to specific positions
- **As a teaching tool**: The eight-point template can be used to teach any position in a structured way: goal, controls, threats, opponent goals, defense, offense, transitions, submissions
