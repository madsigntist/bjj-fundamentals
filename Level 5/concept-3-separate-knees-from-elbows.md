# Separate Knees from Elbows

---

## Definition

Separating knees from elbows means breaking the guard player's elbow-knee connection: the defensive structure that keeps upper and lower body linked. When the guard player's elbows and knees are together, they can re-guard, build frames, and maintain a compact defensive shape. When they are separated, the guard collapses and the pass becomes available.

---

## Why It Matters

The elbow-knee connection is the single most important defensive structure in jiu jitsu from bottom (Level 2: elbow-knee connection). It is what turns space into guard. It is what prevents the pass from completing. It is the gateway the bottom player uses for guard recovery.

From the passer's perspective, this means the elbow-knee connection is the single most important thing to defeat.

Without separating knees from elbows:

- The guard player can always re-insert a knee between the bodies
- Every pass attempt is blocked by the same compact defensive shell
- The passer clears the legs but the elbows redirect whatever gets through
- Passing becomes a fight against a wall that keeps rebuilding itself

With this separation:

- The guard player loses the ability to re-guard: their upper and lower body are disconnected
- The pass completes because there is nothing linking the frame barrier to the leg barrier
- The compact shell is broken: the guard player is stretched, flat, and vulnerable
- Once separated, the guard player's defensive options drop dramatically

If this is ignored, the student clears legs and still cannot pass because the elbows are right there to re-frame and re-insert the knees. They are fighting the symptom (the legs) instead of the cause (the elbow-knee link).

---

## Core Idea

The guard lives in the connection between elbows and knees. Break that connection and the guard dies.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**Why elbows and knees matter together.** When the guard player's elbows are close to their knees, they have a compact defensive shape. Their torso is curled, their knees are up, and their elbows are inside the knees. From this shape:

- They can frame against your chest with their forearms (elbows)
- They can shield your advance with their knees and shins (knees)
- They can transition from frame to knee shield to foot on hip seamlessly because there is no gap

This is the retention system from Level 4 in its most effective form: all three layers (feet, knees, hips/frames) compressed into a tight package where each one reinforces the others.

**What separation looks like.** When the elbows and knees are separated, the guard player's body is extended or twisted. Their upper body is pulled one direction while their lower body is pulled or pinned another. From this position:

- Frames cannot reach the approaching body because the elbows are too far from the action
- Knees cannot re-insert because the hips are turned or pinned away from the elbows
- The retention layers collapse because the connection between them is broken

**Methods of separation.**

_Method 1: Push the knees away from the elbows._ Drive the guard player's knees toward the mat on one side while your upper body advances on the other side. The torreando pass does this (pushing the legs to one side while your body goes to the other. The stack pass does this vertically) driving the knees toward the opponent's head while their shoulders stay on the mat.

_Method 2: Pull the elbows away from the knees._ Control the guard player's upper body (collar grip, arm drag, head control) and pull or redirect their upper body while their legs are controlled or pinned. This stretches the defensive shape. An arm drag pulls the near arm across the body: now the elbow on that side is far from the knee, and the pass opens on that side.

_Method 3: Turn the hips away from the elbows._ Control the guard player's hips and force rotation. If the hips turn away, the knees go with them: separating from the elbows on the side the passer is advancing. The backstep pass and leg drag both achieve this: turning the opponent's lower body while the upper body stays flat.

_Method 4: Pin one side and advance on the other._ Pin the guard player's near knee to the mat with your shin or hand. Their elbow on that side is now isolated from its partner knee. Advance on the far side where the remaining elbow has no knee support. The knee cut pass uses this: the leading knee pins the bottom leg, separating the near elbow-knee pair and creating a channel to slide through.

**The timing of separation.** Separation is most effective when combined with the gate progression (Concept 2). As you clear each gate (feet, knees), you should be creating separation as part of the clearing. Do not clear the knees and then try to separate (clear the knees in a way that creates separation. The two concepts work together) the gate model tells you which layer to clear, and the separation principle tells you how to clear it in a way that prevents re-guarding.

**Maintaining separation.** Creating separation is not enough: you must maintain it. The guard player will immediately try to reconnect elbows and knees. The moment you create separation, you must advance through the gap before it closes. Grip control, pressure, and speed all contribute to maintaining separation long enough to complete the pass.

---

## Key Principles

- The elbow-knee connection is the foundation of guard retention: defeat it to pass
- Separation means the elbows and knees are no longer able to work together
- Four methods: push knees away, pull elbows away, turn hips, or pin one side and advance on the other
- Create separation as part of clearing each gate, not as a separate step
- Once separation is created, advance immediately: the guard player will try to reconnect
- A stretched or twisted body cannot re-guard effectively
- If the elbows and knees are together, the pass is blocked no matter what else you do

---

## Common Mistakes

- Trying to pass against a connected, compact guard player without creating separation
- Creating separation momentarily but not advancing through the gap fast enough
- Focusing only on beating the legs without addressing the elbows that will reframe
- Ignoring upper body control: the elbows stay free and reconnect to the knees
- Releasing grip control after creating separation, allowing the guard player to curl back up
- Not recognizing when the guard player has re-established elbow-knee connection mid-pass

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Knee Cut Pass (Pin and Advance)

- Lead knee slices across the opponent's thigh, pinning the bottom leg to the mat
- This separates the bottom-side knee from the bottom-side elbow: the connection on that side is broken
- Your crossface controls the head, preventing the upper body from curling toward the pinned knee
- Advance through the gap created by the separation on the near side

### Torreando Pass (Push Knees Away)

- Grip both pant legs and push the knees to the mat on one side
- The knees are now far from the elbows on the passing side: the guard player is twisted
- Sprint past the legs to the opposite side
- The separation between knees (pushed away) and elbows (left behind) is what allows the pass to complete

### Stack Pass (Vertical Separation)

- Drive the guard player's knees toward their head, folding them over
- Their lower body is compressed toward their upper body, but their shoulders are flat on the mat
- Circle their legs to one side: the hips turn while the shoulders stay flat
- The diagonal twist separates the knees from the far-side elbows, and the pass completes to that side

### Leg Drag Pass (Turn Hips)

- Control one leg and drag it across the opponent's body
- Their hips are forced to turn: the knees follow the drag direction
- The elbows on the far side are now separated from the knees by the hip rotation
- Advance to the far side where the elbows cannot reach the knees

---

## Simple Mental Model

A zipper. When the elbows and knees are together, the zipper is closed and the guard is sealed. Your job is to unzip it: pull one end away from the other. Once it is open, step through before they zip it back up.

## Optional

### Drills

**Separation Recognition Drill**

- Guard player starts in a compact defensive position (knees up, elbows in)
- Passer identifies which side has the weakest elbow-knee connection
- Call out "left" or "right" and explain why
- No passing, just reading the connection and finding the opening

**Pin-and-Pass Drill**

- Start in the guard player's half guard with knee shield
- Passer's goal: pin the near knee to the mat (separating it from the elbow) and advance
- Guard player's goal: maintain elbow-knee connection on the shield side
- Isolates the specific battle for separation in a common passing scenario

**Reconnection Prevention Drill**

- Passer creates separation (any method) and then holds for 5 seconds
- Guard player tries to reconnect elbows and knees during those 5 seconds
- If the guard player reconnects, the round resets
- Builds the habit of maintaining separation once created

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on recognizing the elbow-knee connection, understanding why it blocks passes, and practicing the methods for creating separation.

At higher levels, separation becomes the organizing principle for the entire passing game. Every advanced pass (berimbolo counter, leg weave, bodylock pass) is fundamentally an elbow-knee separation technique. The specific grips and movements differ, but the principle is the same. The student who understands separation can analyze any pass they see and identify the moment where elbows and knees are split.
