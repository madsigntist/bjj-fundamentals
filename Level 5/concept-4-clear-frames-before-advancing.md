# Clear Frames Before Advancing

---

## Definition

Clearing frames before advancing means removing the guard player's skeletal barriers (forearm frames, stiff-arms, and posting hands) before moving your body past them. Frames are structural blocks the bottom player uses to create distance and redirect your weight. If you advance into a frame without removing it, the frame does the bottom player's work for them: it holds you in place, creates space for re-guarding, and burns your energy while costing them almost nothing.

---

## Why It Matters

Frames are the cheapest, most efficient defensive tool the guard player has. A single forearm across the throat stops a much larger passer from completing the pass. A stiff-arm to the hip prevents the passer from closing distance. Frames let the guard player defend without using muscles: they use bone structure and alignment to transfer force directly into the mat or into the passer's body.

Without clearing frames:

- the pass stalls at the last mile: the passer clears the legs but runs into a wall of forearms and elbows
- the passer burns energy trying to drive through skeletal structures (bone doesn't fatigue)
- the guard player uses the distance created by frames to re-insert knees and recover guard
- every positional advance is temporary because the bottom player frames back to space immediately

With frame clearing:

- the passer removes the guard player's ability to create distance at the moment of the pass
- the pass completes cleanly to a control position without a scramble
- the guard player is flat, heavy, and compressed with no structural barriers remaining
- energy is conserved because the passer does not fight against structural resistance

This is the bridge between beating the legs (Concepts 2-3) and establishing top control. Many students can beat feet, clear knees, and separate elbows from knees, but then stall because a single forearm frame blocks the final advance. Frame clearing is what finishes the pass.

---

## Core Idea

Never advance your body into a frame. Remove the frame first, then advance into the space where the frame was.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**What frames are.** Frames are skeletal structures (forearms, straight arms, or posted hands) that the bottom player places between their body and the passer's body. A frame works because it transfers force from the passer's weight through the bottom player's bones into the mat. The bottom player does not need to push: they hold a structural position and let the passer's own pressure do the work of keeping space open.

Common frames during passing:

- **Forearm across the throat or neck**: Creates an angled barrier that redirects the crossface and prevents head control
- **Stiff-arm to the hip or shoulder**: Keeps the passer at long range and prevents upper body contact
- **Elbow on the inside of the knee**: Supports the knee shield and makes it structural rather than muscular (ties into the elbow-knee connection from Concept 3)
- **Posted hand on the mat**: Creates a base for hip escape or guard recovery
- **Cross-face frame (forearm across the passer's face)**: Redirects the passer's head position and disrupts their control angle

**Why you cannot advance through frames.** Trying to push through a well-placed frame is fighting bone structure with muscle. The bottom player's forearm is a rigid beam supported by the mat on one end and your body on the other. Driving harder only loads the beam more, and the more force you apply, the more stable the frame becomes. This is the opposite of what you want. The passer's energy is finite; the bottom player's bone structure is not.

Additionally, advancing into a frame telegraphs the pass direction. If you drive into a forearm on the left side of your neck, the bottom player knows you are going left and positions their hips to re-guard on that side. The frame is not just a barrier: it is an early warning system.

**Methods of clearing frames.**

_Method 1: Redirect the frame._ Change the angle of the frame so it no longer blocks your path. A forearm across the throat blocks a straight crossface, but if you change your angle (shift your hips, move your head to the other side), the frame is now pointing the wrong direction. You do not remove the frame; you make it irrelevant by changing the line of attack.

_Method 2: Strip the frame._ Use grip control to physically remove the frame. Pin the framing hand to the mat, swim under the forearm, or peel the hand off your body and control the wrist. Once the frame is removed, advance immediately before it is replaced. Stripping is direct but requires good timing: strip and advance in one motion.

_Method 3: Collapse the frame._ Change the frame's alignment so it loses structural integrity. A forearm frame works because it is at the correct angle to transfer force. If you shift the angle (push the elbow past the line of the shoulder, or drive the wrist past the center of the body) the frame collapses because the bones are no longer aligned. A collapsed frame cannot hold weight.

_Method 4: Go around the frame._ If a frame blocks one path, take another path. A stiff-arm on your right hip prevents you from passing right, so pass left. A forearm on the throat prevents the crossface, so underhook instead. This is not avoiding the frame; it is making the frame defend the wrong side while you pass on the open side.

_Method 5: Use the frame against the bottom player._ If the bottom player posts a hand on the mat to hip escape, that hand is committed and cannot be used for anything else. Pin it. If they frame against your head, their elbow is exposed: swim under for the underhook. Every frame creates a commitment that opens something else.

**When to clear frames.** Frame clearing happens at the transition between clearing the legs and establishing top control. In the gate model (Concept 2):

- Feet → Knees → Hips: these are the gates where the legs are the primary obstacles
- **Hips → Upper Body**: this is the gate where frames become the primary obstacle

The hips-to-upper-body transition is where most passes die. The passer has done the hard work of clearing the legs, the hips are partially passed, but frames hold off the final advance. Frame clearing is the skill that finishes this transition.

**Sequence: clear, then advance.** The fundamental pattern is always the same:

1. Identify the frame that is blocking the advance
2. Clear the frame using any of the five methods
3. Advance immediately into the space the frame was protecting
4. Establish control before a new frame can be placed

Step 3 is critical: the gap between clearing and advancing must be as small as possible. The bottom player will replace frames constantly. Clearing a frame and pausing gives them time to build a new one. Clear and move in one motion.

---

## Key Principles

- Never advance your body into a frame: remove it first
- Frames are skeletal, not muscular: you cannot outmuscle bone structure
- Every frame has a structural angle: change the angle and the frame fails
- Clear and advance in one motion: pausing allows replacement frames
- Frames are commitments: the arm that frames cannot do anything else, which creates openings
- Frame clearing is what finishes the pass after the legs have been beaten
- If the pass stalls at the last moment, a frame you did not address is the likely cause

---

## Common Mistakes

- Trying to drive through a forearm frame with pressure: loading the frame makes it stronger
- Clearing a frame and then pausing: the bottom player builds a new frame in the gap
- Ignoring upper body frames because the legs are already cleared: the pass dies at the last step
- Treating all frames the same: different frames require different clearing methods
- Clearing the frame but losing hip control: the bottom player hip escapes during the clearing attempt
- Not recognizing that a stalling pass is caused by an uncleared frame

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Knee Cut to Side Control (Strip and Advance)

- Knee is sliding through, hips are partially past the guard player's legs
- Guard player places a forearm frame across the throat, blocking the crossface
- Do not drive into the frame: use the far hand to strip the framing wrist to the mat
- Simultaneously drive the crossface and settle into side control
- The strip and advance happen in one beat, not two

### Torreando to Side Control (Redirect)

- Legs are passed to one side after the torreando grip throw
- Guard player posts a stiff-arm against the approaching shoulder to create distance
- Instead of driving through the stiff-arm, circle the upper body to the opposite angle
- The stiff-arm is now pointing at empty space: advance on the new angle
- The frame was not removed, it was made irrelevant

### Half Guard to Mount (Collapse the Frame)

- Bottom player has a forearm frame on the hip, maintaining spacing in half guard
- Drive the framing elbow past the line of the shoulder: push it toward their far ear
- The frame collapses because the bones are no longer aligned to bear weight
- Fill the space the frame was protecting and advance to mount

### Bodylock Pass (Use the Frame Against Them)

- Guard player posts a hand on the mat to hip escape out of the bodylock
- That posted hand is committed: it cannot frame against the pass
- Pin the posted hand to the mat with your near hand
- Now both of the guard player's arms are accounted for: one pinned, one trapped by your body
- Complete the pass over the pinned side

## Simple Mental Model

A door. A frame is a door between you and the pass. You would not run through a closed door: you would open it first, then walk through. Every frame is a door. Open it, then advance.

## Optional

### Drills

**Frame Identification Drill**

- Passer starts in a partially passed position (past the legs, not yet settled)
- Guard player places one frame at a time in different positions
- Passer calls out the frame location and chooses a clearing method
- No live rolling, just identification and method selection

**Clear-and-Advance Drill**

- Start with the passer in half guard top
- Guard player places a forearm frame on the throat
- Passer must strip the frame and advance to side control in one motion
- If there is any pause between the strip and the advance, reset
- Builds the habit of treating clearing and advancing as one action

**Frame Replacement Drill**

- Passer must pass from guard to side control
- Guard player cannot use legs: only upper body frames
- Guard player replaces frames as fast as possible
- Passer practices clearing sequential frames without stalling
- Develops the ability to handle multiple frames during one pass

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on recognizing that frames are what block the final phase of the pass, and on developing the habit of clearing before advancing.

At higher levels, frame clearing integrates with submission setups. A frame that is stripped opens an arm for attack. A frame that is collapsed exposes the neck. The student begins to see frame clearing not just as a passing tool but as the beginning of the next attack. The frame is not just an obstacle: it is an opportunity the bottom player has handed you by committing their arm to a fixed position.
