# Level 5 Integration - Passing Systems

---

## Level Goal

Systematically defeat guard structures.

---

## Level Outcomes

- Pass guard consistently
- Avoid being re-guarded
- Establish top control after the pass

---

## Concept Summary

| #   | Concept                               | Core Lesson                                                                                                            |
| --- | ------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Control Before Passing                | Establish dominant grips, posture, and base before attempting a pass - rushing without control wastes energy and fails |
| 2   | Beat Feet → Knees → Hips → Upper Body | Guard is four sequential gates; clear each one in order and consolidate before advancing to the next                   |
| 3   | Separate Knees from Elbows            | The elbow-knee connection is the foundation of guard retention - break it and the guard collapses                      |
| 4   | Clear Frames Before Advancing         | Never advance into a frame; remove it first, then fill the space it was protecting                                     |
| 5   | Pressure vs Movement Passing          | Every pass is pressure, movement, or both - read the situation to choose the right approach, and switch when it stalls |

---

## How the Concepts Connect

Level 5 transforms the top player from someone who reacts to the guard into someone who systematically dismantles it. The five concepts form a sequential passing system where each concept addresses a specific phase of the pass.

**Control Before Passing** is the launch pad. Before any pass attempt begins, the passer must establish the conditions for success: dominant grips, stable posture, and a solid base. Without this foundation, every subsequent concept fails - you cannot work through the gates if you have no grips, you cannot separate elbows from knees if you have no control of the legs, and you cannot clear frames if your own posture is broken. Control is what converts the chaos of an open guard engagement into a structured passing opportunity.

**Beat Feet → Knees → Hips → Upper Body** is the roadmap. Once control is established, the passer needs a systematic path through the guard. The gate model provides this path: four sequential layers, each cleared and consolidated before moving to the next. This prevents the most common passing error - jumping past gates and leaving defensive layers intact behind you. The gate model tells you where you are in the pass and what to do next at every stage.

**Separate Knees from Elbows** is the key that unlocks the gates. The gate model tells you to clear each layer in sequence, but the elbow-knee connection is what makes those layers resilient. The guard player's ability to re-guard depends on keeping elbows and knees working together. As the passer clears each gate, they must create separation between the upper and lower body - pushing knees one direction, controlling elbows the other, turning hips, or pinning one side to advance on the other. Without separation, cleared gates reclose.

**Clear Frames Before Advancing** is the finisher. After the legs are beaten and the elbows are separated from the knees, the last obstacle is the guard player's skeletal frames - forearm barriers, stiff-arms, and posts that hold off the final advance. This is where most passes die: the hard work of clearing legs is done, but a single forearm across the throat blocks the completion. Frame clearing is the bridge between beating the guard and establishing the control position.

**Pressure vs Movement Passing** is the strategic lens. It sits on top of the other four concepts and determines how each one is executed. Control before passing looks different in a pressure game (heavy grips, slow settling) than in a movement game (quick grips, immediate redirection). Gate clearing looks different with pressure (grinding through each layer) than with movement (sprinting past layers). Separation and frame clearing each have pressure variants and movement variants. This concept gives the student the ability to adapt the entire system to the situation in front of them.

The relationship flows like this:

**Control Before Passing** (establish the foundation)
→ **Beat Feet → Knees → Hips → Upper Body** (work through the gates)
→→ **Separate Knees from Elbows** (break the connection that holds each gate together)
→→→ **Clear Frames Before Advancing** (remove the last barrier to completion)
→→→→ **Pressure vs Movement** (choose how to execute each step)

## The Integrated Passing Sequence

When passing the guard, all five concepts execute together:

1. **Establish control** (Control Before Passing) - Before any movement, secure grips, stabilize posture, and establish base. Strip the guard player's grips first, then build your own grip configuration. Do not advance until the pre-pass checklist is satisfied.

2. **Identify the current gate** (Beat Feet → Knees → Hips → Upper Body) - Read where you are in the passing sequence. Are the feet still active? Are the knees blocking? Are the hips still mobile? Identify the current gate and address it before looking past it.

3. **Clear the gate by separating** (Separate Knees from Elbows) - As you clear each gate, create separation between the guard player's upper and lower body. Do not just beat the legs - beat the legs in a way that disconnects them from the elbows. Push knees away, pull elbows away, turn hips, or pin one side.

4. **Remove frames and advance** (Clear Frames Before Advancing) - Once the legs are past and elbows are separated from knees, address the remaining frames. Strip, redirect, collapse, or go around. Advance into the cleared space immediately - do not pause between clearing and advancing.

5. **Choose and adapt the approach** (Pressure vs Movement) - Throughout the entire sequence, operate in the right mode. Read the guard player's defensive approach and choose pressure or movement accordingly. When one approach stalls, switch to the other. Use pressure to create movement opportunities and movement to create pressure opportunities.

## Positional Application

### Passing Closed Guard (Full Integration)

- **Control**: Break the posture battle first. Hands on the chest or hips, posture tall. Strip the guard player's collar grips, sleeve grips, and wrist controls. Establish your own grips - hands on the biceps or gi pants at the knees. Do not try to open the guard until your posture and grips are set.
- **Gates**: The first gate is unique in closed guard - the legs are locked. Open the guard (stand up, knee-in-the-middle, or stacking method). Once open, the feet go to the floor or to your hips - now the normal gate sequence begins. Clear feet (control the ankles or pants), clear knees (push them aside or split them), advance through hips.
- **Separation**: As you open the guard and begin passing, force the knees away from the elbows. If the guard player tries to re-close or re-insert a knee, they need their elbow close to their knee. Push the legs to one side while your upper body goes the other - the elbow-knee connection breaks on the passing side.
- **Frames**: After clearing the legs, the guard player will frame against your head, neck, or shoulder. Strip the crossface-side frame first - this is the one that blocks the pass completion. Pin the framing wrist and drive the crossface simultaneously.
- **Approach**: Guard break and initial pass - pressure (slow, controlled, weight-based opening). If the guard player is very flexible and keeps re-closing, switch to movement - stand up quickly and use torreando-style speed to get past the legs before they re-close. Finish with pressure to settle into side control.

### Passing Open Guard / De La Riva (Full Integration)

- **Control**: The guard player has a DLR hook and grips. Do not try to pass with their grips intact. Strip the collar grip first, then the sleeve grip. Control the DLR leg at the ankle. Establish your own pants grip on the free leg. Now you have control - the guard player's grip system is broken and yours is established.
- **Gates**: The DLR hook is Gate 1 (feet). Remove the hook by stepping back and clearing the foot, or by squatting and peeling the hook. Once the hook is cleared, the knees are Gate 2 - control the legs and prevent knee shield re-insertion. Advance through the hips (Gate 3) with your pass.
- **Separation**: As you clear the DLR hook and advance, push the knees to the mat on one side (torreando) or drag one leg across the body (leg drag). Both methods separate the knees from the elbows on the passing side. The guard player's compact defensive shell is broken.
- **Frames**: After the torreando throw or leg drag, the guard player frantically frames - forearm to the hip, stiff-arm to the shoulder. You are past the legs but not yet in side control. Go around the hip frame by circling your hips to the opposite angle. Or strip the shoulder frame and drop your weight. Clear and advance in one motion.
- **Approach**: DLR is a long-range guard - movement passing is the natural first approach. Torreando, leg drag, and x-pass all exploit speed. If the guard player is very grippy and keeps re-establishing the hook, switch to pressure - smash the DLR leg to the mat, flatten, and grind through with a knee cut. Use the failed movement attempt to disguise the pressure switch.

### Passing Half Guard (Full Integration)

- **Control**: Establish crossface and underhook - the two essential controls for half guard top. If the guard player has the underhook, you must recover it before passing. Strip their underhook by swimming inside, whizzering, or framing and re-pummeling. Do not try to pass with a bottom underhook against you.
- **Gates**: In half guard, the feet and knees are partially cleared - the opponent has one leg trapped. The remaining gate is the knee shield (Gate 2) and the hip (Gate 3). Clear the knee shield by driving it to the mat with hip pressure or by backstopping. Then address the trapped leg - extract it to complete the hips-to-upper-body transition.
- **Separation**: The knee shield is the critical elbow-knee connection in half guard. The bottom player's near elbow supports the knee shield - together they form a wall. Separate them by driving the crossface (moving the elbow away from the knee) while your hips drive the knee toward the mat. Once the shield collapses and the elbow is far from the knee, the pass opens.
- **Frames**: As you flatten the guard player and extract the leg, they will frame across your neck or shoulder. The crossface controls the near-side frame by default. The far-side frame must be stripped - pin the wrist to the mat as you settle. If you ignore the far-side frame, they hip escape and re-guard.
- **Approach**: Half guard is a close-range position - pressure is the dominant approach. Heavy crossface, flattening weight, incremental advancement. If the guard player is very active with underhook recovery and knee re-insertion, switch to movement - backstep out of half guard entirely and re-engage from a standing position, then use speed to pass the newly opened guard before they can re-establish half guard grips.

### Passing Butterfly Guard (Full Integration)

- **Control**: Control the shoulders or collar ties to prevent the guard player from sitting up and reaching for underhooks. If they have an underhook, they are dangerous - address it immediately by whizzering or framing. Establish your own connection: collar ties, bicep control, or pants grips on the legs.
- **Gates**: Butterfly hooks are Gate 2 (knees). The feet are inside - they become the hooks themselves. To clear the hooks, drive the guard player flat to the mat (removes the elevation angle of the hooks). Then clear the hooks by stepping over them or sliding the knees to the mat.
- **Separation**: As you flatten the guard player, their elbows and knees separate naturally - a flat guard player's elbows are on the mat and the knees lose their elevation. Accelerate this by pinning one knee to the mat while controlling the opposite-side upper body. The diagonal separation makes re-guarding impossible.
- **Frames**: Once flat with hooks cleared, the guard player frames hard with forearms. This is their last defense. Collapse the frames by driving elbows past the line of the shoulders, or swim under for underhooks. Advance to side control as the frames clear.
- **Approach**: Against an active butterfly player who is sitting up and reaching, pressure first - drive them flat, remove the sit-up posture that powers the hooks. Once flat, if they are still mobile, switch to movement - quick torreando or leg drag while they are on their back. The pressure forced them down; the movement finishes the pass while they are displaced.

## Decision-Making Checklist

When a pass is not working, run this diagnostic:

| Check       | Question                                                | If No...                                                                    |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Control     | Do I have dominant grips and stable posture?            | Stop the pass - establish control first                                     |
| Gate        | Do I know which gate I am at?                           | Read the position - identify the current layer before advancing             |
| Separation  | Are elbows and knees separated on the passing side?     | Create separation before trying to advance - the pass will stall without it |
| Frames      | Have I cleared the frames in my path?                   | Strip, redirect, collapse, or go around - do not advance into the frame     |
| Approach    | Am I using the right category (pressure or movement)?   | Switch approaches - the current one may not fit the situation               |
| Combination | Have I tried switching approaches?                      | Use pressure to set up movement, or movement to set up pressure             |
| Re-guard    | Did the opponent re-guard? Which gate did they recover? | Re-establish control and work back through the gate sequence                |

## Drills

**Full Passing Integration (6-Minute Rounds)**

- Start standing or kneeling in the guard player's open guard.
- Passer must use all five concepts: establish control, work through the gates, create separation, clear frames, and choose the right approach.
- Guard player works full retention. After each round, the passer identifies which concept broke down first when the pass failed.

**Gate Identification Drill**

- Passer works to pass at 50% intensity.
- At any point, the coach calls "Freeze."
- Passer must identify: which gate am I at? What is blocking me? What do I do next?
- Reset and continue. Builds conscious awareness of the gate sequence during live passing.

**Pressure-to-Movement Switching Drill**

- Passer starts a pressure pass (knee cut, over-under, or bodylock).
- At any point, the coach calls "Switch."
- Passer immediately transitions to a movement pass (torreando, leg drag, or x-pass).
- On the next call, switch back to pressure.
- Builds the ability to change approaches mid-pass without losing control.

**Frame Clearing Under Pressure**

- Start with the passer past the legs but not yet in side control.
- Guard player uses only upper body frames (no leg re-insertion).
- Passer must clear the frames and establish side control within 15 seconds.
- Guard player replaces frames as fast as possible.
- Isolates the frame-clearing phase of the pass and builds urgency.

**Re-Guard Prevention Drill**

- Passer completes a pass to side control.
- Guard player immediately tries to re-guard.
- Passer must maintain the position for 10 seconds and then re-pass if the guard is recovered.
- Builds awareness of what causes re-guarding and how to prevent it.

## Relationship to Other Levels

**Level 1 (Mechanical Foundations)** provides the posture, base, and frames that the passer uses throughout. The passer's posture during Control Before Passing is Level 1 posture. The passer's base during pressure passing is Level 1 base. The frames the passer encounters and must clear are the guard player using Level 1 frames.

**Level 2 (Defense and Escape)** is the opponent's toolkit. Everything the guard player does to survive the pass - elbow-knee connection, frame creation, guard recovery - is Level 2 defense. Understanding Level 2 tells the passer what the opponent will try and why. The concepts in Level 5 are direct counters to Level 2 defenses: Separate Knees from Elbows defeats the elbow-knee connection. Clear Frames Before Advancing defeats frame creation. The gate model defeats guard recovery by consolidating each advance.

**Level 3 (Positional Control)** is what comes after the pass. The entire purpose of Level 5 is to get to a Level 3 position - side control, mount, or back control with pinning mechanics, weight distribution, and connection. A pass that does not transition cleanly into a Level 3 control position is incomplete. Frame clearing and immediate advancement are what bridge the pass into the pin.

**Level 4 (Guard Systems)** is the direct opponent of Level 5. Level 4 teaches the guard player to manage distance, maintain connections, retain layers, and choose attacks. Level 5 teaches the passer to defeat all of those systems. Every concept in Level 5 has a direct counterpart in Level 4: the gate model defeats retention layers. Separation defeats the connection points. Control Before Passing defeats off-balancing by stabilizing the passer's base and posture. The two levels are two sides of the same battle.

**Level 6 (Submission Systems)** is where the pass connects to the finish. Frame clearing opens submission opportunities - a stripped arm is an exposed arm. Pressure passing fatigues the opponent, making them vulnerable to submissions. The pass is not the end; it is the transition to a control position from which submissions become available.

---

## Summary

Level 5 is a complete passing system. Control before passing gives you the foundation. The gate model gives you the roadmap. Separation gives you the key to unlock each gate. Frame clearing gives you the finishing tool. Pressure vs movement gives you the strategic adaptability to use all of it in the right way for the right situation.

The guard does not fail because of one big move. It fails because the passer executes a systematic sequence - establishing control, working through the gates, breaking the elbow-knee connection, clearing the frames, and adapting the approach. When all five concepts work together, the guard is dismantled layer by layer until there is nothing left to defend.
