# Control Before Passing

---

## Definition

Control before passing means establishing dominant grips, posture, and positioning before attempting to move past the guard. The pass does not begin with movement — it begins with control. You must own the exchange before you try to advance through it.

---

## Why It Matters

The most common reason guard passes fail is that the passer rushes. They see a gap, lunge for it, and the guard player re-guards because the passer had no control infrastructure in place. The pass attempt itself creates the space and momentum the guard player needs to recover.

Without control before passing:

- every pass attempt is a gamble — sometimes it works, usually it does not
- the guard player dictates the exchange because they have grips and connections while the passer does not
- failed pass attempts give the guard player sweep and submission opportunities
- passing becomes exhausting because each attempt starts from zero

With control before passing:

- the pass begins from a position of advantage — grips established, guard player's structure disrupted
- failed pass attempts do not cost position because the control remains
- the guard player is reactive instead of proactive
- passing becomes systematic instead of opportunistic

If this is ignored, the student treats passing as a single explosive movement instead of a structured process. They wonder why their passes only work against lower-level opponents who cannot retain guard.

---

## Core Idea

Win the grip fight. Establish your controls. Then pass. Never in the other order.

---

## Detailed Explanation

Guard passing is a multi-phase process. Most students focus on the passing movement — the actual step or slide or smash that gets them past the legs. But that movement is the last phase, not the first. The first phase is establishing control.

**What control means from the top.** When you are standing or kneeling in front of a guard player, they have grips, hooks, and structure. Their feet are on your hips. Their hands are on your collar and sleeves. Their knees are shielding their body. They have a system of connections (Level 4) designed to manage distance, off-balance you, and retain guard.

Control means systematically dismantling that system before you attempt to pass.

Specifically:

- *Strip their grips.* The guard player's collar grip breaks your posture. Their sleeve grip controls your posting arm. These grips are the steering wheel of their guard. Remove them and the guard loses direction.
- *Establish your grips.* Once their grips are stripped, establish your own. Pant grips at the knees give you control of their leg position. Collar grips give you posture and directional control. Sleeve grips prevent them from re-gripping. Your grips are your control infrastructure — they are what allow the pass to happen methodically.
- *Control their legs.* The guard player's legs are their primary weapons (Level 4 — inside leg position). Before passing, you must have a strategy for their legs. Pin a knee. Push a foot off your hip. Stack the legs to one side. If their legs are free and active, you are not ready to pass.
- *Establish your posture and base.* Your posture determines whether you can resist their off-balancing. Your base determines whether you can resist their sweeps. Before passing, ensure you are postured (head up, spine straight, weight centered) and based (knees wide, hips low, weight balanced).

**The sequence.** The pre-pass control phase follows a general order:

1. Defend against their guard offense — block sweeps, resist off-balancing, prevent submission entries
2. Strip their grips — remove the connections they need for offense and retention
3. Establish your grips — build your own control infrastructure
4. Control or neutralize their legs — address their primary defensive weapons
5. Choose and execute the pass

Steps 1-4 can take time. This is not wasted time — it is preparation that makes step 5 succeed. Rushing to step 5 without completing 1-4 is why passes fail.

**Patience from the top.** Just as Level 3 taught patience in top control (preventing movement before attacking), Level 5 teaches patience in passing. The guard player wants you to rush. When you rush, you create momentum they can redirect. When you are patient and methodical, they must deal with your control while you work through the phases.

**Control survives failed passes.** When you attempt a pass from a position of control and the pass fails, you still have your grips. You still have posture. You still have their legs managed. You are back to step 4, not back to step 1. This is the crucial advantage of control-first passing. Without it, a failed pass resets everything — the guard player re-establishes their entire system and you start from scratch.

---

## Key Principles

- Strip the guard player's grips before attempting to pass
- Establish your own grips — they are your control infrastructure for the pass
- Control or neutralize the guard player's legs before moving
- Maintain posture and base throughout the control phase
- Patience from the top is a weapon — rushing serves the guard player
- Control survives failed passes — you do not reset to zero
- The pass is the last phase, not the first

---

## Common Mistakes

- Rushing to pass without establishing grips or controlling the legs
- Allowing the guard player to maintain all their connections while attempting to pass
- Ignoring the guard player's grips and trying to muscle through
- Abandoning posture to reach for a pass — the guard player sweeps or submits
- Treating passing as a single movement instead of a multi-phase process
- Getting frustrated by the control phase and rushing to the pass too early
- Not re-establishing control after a failed pass attempt

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Passing Closed Guard

- **Control phase**: Establish posture first — hands on the hips or belt, head up, spine straight. Resist the guard player's attempts to break your posture (collar pulls, hip bumps). Once postured, strip their collar grip, then work to open the closed guard (standing up, knee in the middle, wedge pass position).
- **Pass only after**: Posture is established, their collar grip is stripped, and the closed guard is opened with your hands controlling their legs.
- **Without control**: You try to open the guard while they have collar grips and broken posture — they sweep or submit you.

### Passing Open Guard (Standing)

- **Control phase**: Grip their pants at the knees to control leg movement. Strip their sleeve grips so they cannot redirect you. Push their feet off your hips. Establish your own collar or lapel grip for directional control.
- **Pass only after**: Their feet are off your hips, you have pant grips controlling their knees, and their primary upper body grips are stripped.
- **Without control**: You try to step around their legs while they have feet on your hips and sleeve grips — they off-balance you, re-guard, or sweep.

### Passing Half Guard (Kneeling)

- **Control phase**: Establish crossface to control the head. Kill the underhook — either by flattening them with pressure or by switching to an overhook. Pin the near hip to prevent the shrimp. Establish your own underhook if possible.
- **Pass only after**: Crossface is in, underhook is neutralized, and the opponent is flattened.
- **Without control**: You try to extract your leg from half guard while they have an underhook and knee shield — they sweep or take the back.

---

## Simple Mental Model

Two phases. Preparation and execution. Spend 80% of your effort on preparation. The execution takes care of itself.

## Optional

### Drills

**Grip Strip Drill**

- Start in the guard player's closed guard or open guard with their full grip set
- Your only goal: strip all their grips and establish two of your own within 60 seconds
- No passing allowed — control phase only
- Builds the habit of winning the grip fight before attempting the pass

**Posture Retention Drill**

- Start in closed guard. Guard player tries to break your posture at 70% effort.
- Your only goal: maintain posture for 90 seconds
- No passing attempts — just posture and base
- Builds the structural foundation that the pass is built on

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on understanding that passing is a process that begins with control, not movement. The student practices the pre-pass phases: grip stripping, grip establishment, leg control, and posture maintenance.

At higher levels, the control phase compresses. Advanced passers establish control quickly because they recognize grip patterns immediately and have efficient responses. They can sometimes combine the control phase and the pass into a single flow. But this fluidity is built on the discipline of understanding each phase separately. Without that foundation, fast passing is just reckless lunging.
