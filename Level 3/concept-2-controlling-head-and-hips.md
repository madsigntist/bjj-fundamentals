# Controlling Head and Hips Together

---

## Definition

Controlling head and hips together means managing the two primary engines of your opponent's movement at the same time. The head directs the body's orientation. The hips generate power and create space. Controlling only one leaves the other free to work.

---

## Why It Matters

Most escape attempts use either the head (turning, posting, looking away to create angles) or the hips (bridging, shrimping, scooting). When you control only one, the opponent compensates with the other.

- Control only the head → the hips escape, they shrimp away
- Control only the hips → the head turns, they create a new angle and recover guard or scramble

The opponent moves in segments. Stop both ends and the middle has nowhere to go. This is why the best top players constantly address both. It is not about choosing between head control and hip control. It is about doing both, simultaneously and continuously.

If this is ignored, the student will feel like they are always one step behind — fixing one problem while another opens.

---

## Core Idea

The body moves from the head and the hips. Control both and the middle cannot go anywhere.

---

## Detailed Explanation

The human body creates movement from two primary regions. The head leads direction changes. Where the head goes, the shoulders and torso follow. The hips generate force. Bridging, shrimping, and turning all originate from hip movement.

**Head control and what it gives you.** When you control the opponent's head, you control where they can look and which direction they can turn. Crossface pressure (driving your shoulder or forearm into the jaw to turn the head away) prevents the opponent from turning into you. If they cannot face you, they cannot build frames toward you, insert a knee, or create the angle needed for most escapes.

Head control also disrupts posture. A head that is turned or compressed to one side makes the spine weak. The opponent cannot generate power from a compromised spine.

**Hip control and what it gives you.** When you control the opponent's hips, you stop the engine. Hip escapes require the hips to slide away — block the hip with your hand, knee, or body and the escape fails. Bridges require the hips to drive upward — keep your weight over the hips or angle your base to absorb the bridge.

Hip control also prevents guard recovery. The shrimp-to-guard sequence requires hip freedom. A blocked hip cannot create the space needed to insert a knee shield or recover full guard.

**Why both matter simultaneously.** The opponent will chain head movement and hip movement together. They bridge to create space, then shrimp. They turn the head to create an angle, then hip escape. If you address only the bridge, the shrimp follows. If you address only the turn, the hip escape follows.

Effective top control means having a solution for both. This does not mean grabbing the head with one hand and the hip with the other at all times. It means being in a position where your structure addresses both. In side control, crossface handles the head while your hip-side arm or knee-hip placement handles the hips. In mount, your seat position controls the hips while your hands or chest control the head and arms.

**The connection between the two.** Head and hip control are not two separate tasks — they are one system. When the head is turned, the hips lose power because the spine is compromised. When the hips are blocked, the head cannot create a useful angle because the body cannot follow. Each reinforces the other.

The best top players think of head-hips as a single control objective, not two independent problems.

---

## Key Principles

- Always have an answer for both the head and the hips
- Crossface controls head direction and disrupts spinal alignment
- Hip blocking (hand, knee, body weight) prevents shrimps and bridges
- Controlling one makes controlling the other easier — they work together
- Head turned away weakens the opponent's entire movement chain
- Hips blocked means guard recovery is shut down
- Think of head-hips as one system, not two separate tasks

---

## Common Mistakes

- Focusing entirely on head control and ignoring hip movement
- Focusing entirely on hip control and letting the opponent turn into you
- Using only hands for control instead of incorporating body position and weight
- Releasing head control to chase a grip or submission
- Letting the opponent get on their side (recovering head position) while addressing the hips
- Treating head and hips as unrelated control problems

---

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Side Control (Top)

- **Head**: Crossface with the near-side arm, driving the opponent's chin away from you. Or underhook the head and pull it tight to your chest.
- **Hips**: Far-side arm blocks the near hip, preventing shrimping. Or knee-hip position where your knee presses into their hip space.
- **Together**: Crossface turns the head away → opponent cannot build frames toward you. Hip block stops the shrimp → opponent cannot recover guard. Both together mean they are stuck flat with no productive movement available.

### Mount (Top)

- **Head**: Chest low, forehead pressure into their chin or chest. Control an arm to prevent frames near the head.
- **Hips**: Sit low, drive hips forward. Heels hooked inside their thighs or grapevine legs to limit hip bridging.
- **Together**: Low base prevents the bridge. Head/arm control prevents the turn. The opponent is left with no productive escape path.

### Back Control

- **Head**: Seatbelt grip combined with head position on the choking side, preventing the opponent from turning away from you.
- **Hips**: Hooks (feet inside the thighs) or body triangle controlling hip movement and preventing the opponent from sliding down.
- **Together**: Head control keeps them from turning to face you. Hip hooks keep them from sliding out the back door.

---

## Simple Mental Model

Two anchors. Head and hips. Lose one, and the ship drifts. Keep both, and they go nowhere.

---

## Optional

### Drills

**Head-Hips Alternating Pressure Drill**

- From side control, have the partner try to escape
- First 30 seconds, focus only on head control — notice what the hips do
- Next 30 seconds, focus only on hip control — notice what the head does
- Final 60 seconds, control both — feel the difference in how little the partner can accomplish

**Mirror Drill**

- From mount, the bottom player alternates between bridging (hips) and trying to turn (head)
- Top player must identify which engine is working and shut it down
- Builds the habit of reading where the movement is coming from

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on understanding why both need to be addressed and practicing the basic positions for controlling each.

At higher levels, this concept feeds directly into transition offense. When you can control both head and hips, you can release one momentarily to set up a submission or transition, knowing you still have the other. The ability to cycle between head emphasis and hip emphasis while maintaining overall control is what makes advanced top games possible.
