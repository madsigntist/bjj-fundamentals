# Inside Leg Position

---

## Definition

Inside leg position means having your legs, feet, or hooks positioned between you and the opponent — inside their arms and legs — rather than outside or underneath. From guard, it means your legs are the first line of engagement, occupying the space between the opponent's limbs and your body.

---

## Why It Matters

Guard is fundamentally a leg game. The guard player's legs are longer and stronger than their arms. They are the primary tools for distance management, off-balancing, sweeping, and retention. But these tools only work when they occupy the inside — the space between you and the opponent.

Without inside leg position:

- the passer gets chest-to-chest and your legs are trapped behind or beneath them
- you have no barrier between the opponent and your hips
- your guard collapses because there is nothing in the way
- sweeps and submissions lose their leverage because your legs cannot engage

With inside leg position:

- your legs create a wall the passer must deal with before they can advance
- you have mechanical advantage — pushing, hooking, redirecting with the strongest part of your body
- guard retention becomes easier because the passer must beat your legs first
- every offensive technique has leverage because your legs are engaged

This is the guard equivalent of the inside position concept from Level 1. The person who controls the inside has the advantage. In guard, your legs are the inside.

If this is ignored, the student has a guard in name only — they are on their back with the opponent on top and no meaningful structure between them.

---

## Core Idea

Whoever has their legs inside, between the bodies, controls the guard exchange.

---

## Detailed Explanation

**What "inside" means from guard.** Imagine a line drawn between your hips and the opponent's hips. Inside leg position means your feet, shins, knees, or hooks are on that line — occupying the corridor between the two bodies. Outside leg position means your legs are wrapped around the opponent (like closed guard with broken posture — which works differently) or dangling to the sides with no engagement.

The most basic inside leg positions:

- _Feet on hips_: Both feet pressing against the opponent's hip bones. Maximum distance enforcement. Pure inside position.
- _Knee shield_: One knee across the opponent's chest or shoulder, shin creating a barrier. Inside position with close-range structure.
- _Butterfly hooks_: Feet hooked inside the opponent's thighs, shins pressed inward. Inside position optimized for elevation and sweep.
- _Shin-to-shin_: Your shin pressed against the opponent's shin, creating connection and inside position from a seated guard.
- _De La Riva hook_: One leg threaded around the outside of the opponent's leg with the foot hooking the inside of the far thigh — a specialized inside position that controls one leg.

**Why inside wins.** The mechanical advantage of inside leg position is leverage. Your legs can push harder than their arms can pull. Your hooks can elevate a body that outweighs you. Your knees can hold a barrier that their chest cannot easily break. When your legs are inside, you have the longer lever ad the stronger muscle group occupying the contested space.

The passer knows this. Every passing system is — at its core — an attempt to get past the guard player's legs. Around them, over them, through them, or under them. Once the passer clears the legs, the guard is passed. Inside leg position is what makes that clearing difficult.

**Inside position as a dynamic state.** Inside leg position is not something you achieve once and hold forever. The passer is constantly working to beat your legs — pushing knees aside, stepping around hooks, pinning shins. The guard player must constantly re-insert, readjust, and reclaim inside position.

This is the dynamic interplay of guard. The passer clears a leg; the guard player re-inserts. The passer pins a knee; the guard player extracts and repositions. The struggle for inside leg position is the struggle for the guard itself.

**The hierarchy of inside leg positions.** Not all inside positions are equal. A rough hierarchy from most controlling to least:

1. Both feet on hips — maximum control, maximum distance
2. Knee shield with underhook — strong barrier with upper body connection
3. Butterfly hooks — good elevation, requires timing
4. Single foot on hip — partial control, one side vulnerable
5. Shin-to-shin — good for transitions, less direct control
6. Legs disengaged — no inside position, guard is in danger

When a higher-level position is lost, the guard player drops to the next level down. When that is lost, they drop again. Guard retention is the process of climbing back up this ladder before it runs out.

**Relationship to passing.** Understanding inside leg position from the guard perspective also reveals the passer's priorities. The passer wants to:

- Strip your feet off their hips
- Flatten your knee shield
- Clear your butterfly hooks
- Get past your shins

Knowing this helps the guard player anticipate. When the passer grabs an ankle, they are attacking your inside position. When they drive into the knee shield, they are trying to flatten it. Recognizing these attacks early is the first step to defending them.

---

## Key Principles

- Your legs are your primary weapons from guard — they must be inside
- Inside leg position means legs between the bodies, occupying the contested space
- Feet on hips is the most fundamental and controlling inside position
- Inside position is dynamic — constantly fought for, lost, and recovered
- Every passing attempt is an attack on your inside leg position
- When you lose a higher-level leg position, drop to the next one immediately
- Legs push harder and reach farther than arms — use them

---

## Common Mistakes

- Letting legs fall to the sides instead of keeping them between the bodies
- Relying on arm grips to hold guard when legs are disengaged
- Not re-inserting legs after the passer clears them
- Keeping legs locked in one position instead of adapting to the passer's movement
- Crossing ankles in open guard (creates a single point of failure the passer can pin)
- Flat on the back with no hip angle — legs cannot reach inside
- Ignoring the hierarchy — jumping to the most complex guard position without first establishing basic inside leg placement

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Open Guard (Feet on Hips)

- Both feet pressing against the hip bones, toes turned slightly outward for grip
- Arms controlling sleeves or collar for complementary grip
- When the passer tries to circle, pivot your hips to track them — feet stay on hips
- If one foot is stripped, immediately replace it or transition to a single-leg inside position (DLR, lasso)

### Half Guard (Knee Shield)

- Bottom knee across the opponent's chest or bicep line, creating a barrier
- Top leg entangling the opponent's far leg (half guard lock)
- The knee shield is your inside structure — if it flattens, the passer achieves chest-to-chest and you lose inside position
- Maintain the shield by driving the knee outward and keeping the elbow connected to the knee

### Butterfly Guard

- Both feet hooked inside the opponent's thighs, shins vertical or angled inward
- Hips underneath the opponent, posture upright or slightly forward
- Hooks provide both inside position and sweep elevation
- If the opponent posts wide to avoid hooks, redirect hooks to the inside of the new base position

### Seated Guard / Shin-to-Shin

- Sitting upright with one shin pressed against the opponent's lead shin
- Inside leg is engaged first — establishing the connection before gripping
- From here, transition to butterfly, single leg X, or DLR by threading the inside leg deeper

## Simple Mental Model

A castle gate. Your legs are the gate. As long as the gate is between you and the invader, the castle holds. When the gate falls, the castle falls. Keep the gate up.

## Optional

### Drills

**Feet-on-Hips Survival Drill**

- Start with both feet on the partner's hips
- Partner tries to clear your feet and pass at 50% effort
- Your only job: keep at least one foot on a hip at all times
- No sweeps, no submissions — just maintain inside leg position
- Three-minute rounds, then switch

**Knee Shield Pressure Test**

- Start in half guard with a knee shield
- Partner applies increasing chest pressure trying to flatten the shield
- Your job: keep the knee shield intact for 60 seconds
- If it flattens, reset and try again
- Builds the strength and positioning awareness to maintain the barrier

**Inside Leg Recovery Drill**

- Start in open guard. Partner strips one foot off the hip.
- You must re-insert a foot to a hip or transition to another inside leg position (DLR hook, butterfly hook, knee shield) within 3 seconds
- Partner continues stripping; you continue re-inserting
- Builds the reflex of recovering inside position when it is lost

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on understanding what inside leg position is, recognizing when you have it and when you have lost it, and practicing the basic positions (feet on hips, knee shield, butterfly hooks).

At higher levels, inside leg position becomes the foundation for complex guard systems. DLR, reverse DLR, X-guard, single leg X, K-guard — all of these are sophisticated inside leg positions. The student who understands the principle can learn any guard variation because they understand what the legs are doing and why. Without this foundation, advanced guards are just shapes without meaning.
