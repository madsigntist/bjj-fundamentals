# Connection Points

---

## Definition

Connection points are the specific grips, hooks, and body contacts the guard player uses to stay attached to the opponent. They are the physical links that make distance management, off-balancing, sweeping, and submitting possible. Without connection, the guard player is just lying on the floor while the opponent stands above them.

---

## Why It Matters

Guard only works when you are connected to the opponent. Every guard technique — every sweep, every submission, every retention movement — requires you to be attached to them in some way. Lose your connections and you lose your guard.

Without connection points:

- the opponent can freely change angles, back away, or rush in on their terms
- you cannot off-balance because you have nothing to push or pull with
- sweeps have no lever to work with — you cannot move someone you are not attached to
- guard retention fails because you cannot feel or redirect their passing energy

With connection points:

- every movement the opponent makes is transmitted through your connections — you feel everything
- you have levers for sweeps, anchors for retention, and entries for submissions
- the opponent must break your connections before they can pass — each connection is a problem they must solve
- your guard becomes a web they must navigate, not an open door they walk through

If this is ignored, the student learns guard shapes but has no ability to make them work. They know what closed guard looks like, but without understanding which connections matter, they cannot control anyone from it.

---

## Core Idea

Your guard is only as strong as your connections. Every grip, hook, and contact is a link in a chain. Lose the links, lose the guard.

---

## Detailed Explanation

Connection points come in three categories: upper body connections, lower body connections, and combined connections. Each serves a different role.

**Upper body connections.** These are grips — your hands controlling the opponent's body. The primary upper body connection points:

- _Collar grip_: Hand gripping the gi collar (or back of neck in no-gi). Controls the head and posture. Pulls the opponent forward or sideways. Essential for off-balancing and choke setup.
- _Sleeve grip_: Hand gripping the gi sleeve at the wrist (or wrist control in no-gi). Controls the opponent's posting arm. Prevents them from basing or defending sweeps. Creates arm isolation for submissions.
- _Underhook_: Arm threaded under the opponent's armpit, hand on the shoulder or back. Controls the opponent's shoulder line and creates inside position on that side. Essential for half guard sweeps and back takes.
- _Overhook_: Arm wrapped over the opponent's arm, clamping it to your body. Traps the arm, prevents posture, and creates submission entries (triangles, omoplatas).
- _Collar tie / Head control_: Hand behind the head pulling it down. Direct posture breaking.

**Lower body connections.** These are hooks, feet, and leg entanglements — your legs controlling the opponent's lower body:

- _Feet on hips_: Distance management and inside position. Direct push-pull control.
- _Butterfly hooks_: Feet hooked inside the thighs. Elevation and sweep platform.
- _DLR hook_: Leg wrapped around the outside of the opponent's lead leg, foot hooking inside the far thigh. Controls one leg and creates off-balance to the hook side.
- _Knee shield_: Shin across the opponent's torso. Barrier and distance enforcer.
- _Half guard leg entanglement_: Legs wrapped around one of the opponent's legs. Prevents the passer from completing to side control and creates sweep base.

**Combined connections.** The strongest guard positions use upper and lower body connections together. The lower body creates structure and leverage. The upper body creates direction and control. Examples:

- _Closed guard with collar and sleeve grip_: Legs lock the waist (lower), collar pulls forward and sleeve controls the post (upper). All threats are available.
- _DLR with collar and ankle grip_: DLR hook controls the lead leg (lower), collar grip pulls the upper body opposite (upper). Creates diagonal off-balance.
- _Half guard with knee shield and underhook_: Knee shield maintains distance (lower), underhook controls the shoulder (upper). Sweeps and back takes open up.

**The connection economy.** You have two hands and two legs — four connection points maximum. The opponent has tools to strip each one. This means you must prioritize. Which connections matter most for what you are trying to do?

- _For retention_: prioritize lower body connections — feet on hips, hooks, knee shield
- _For sweeps_: you need both upper and lower — a grip to direct and a hook to elevate
- _For submissions_: prioritize upper body connections — collar, sleeve, overhook — supported by leg position
- _For off-balancing_: at least one upper and one lower — create opposing forces

When a connection is stripped, replace it immediately or transition to a guard position that works with the remaining connections. Do not fight to keep a dead grip — redirect to a new connection.

**Connections as information.** Like the connection-and-pressure concept from Level 3 (top perspective), connections from guard also serve as sensors. Through your grips, you feel the opponent's weight shifts, posture changes, and directional intentions. A sleeve grip tells you when they are pulling the arm back to strip it. A collar grip tells you when they are posturing up. Hooks tell you when they are shifting weight to pass.

This information drives your decision-making. You do not need to watch the opponent's every move — you feel it through your connections.

---

## Key Principles

- Every guard position requires specific connections to function
- Upper body connections (grips) control direction and posture
- Lower body connections (hooks, feet) control distance, structure, and leverage
- Combined upper-lower connections create the strongest guard positions
- You have four connection points maximum — prioritize based on your goal
- When a connection is stripped, replace it immediately or transition
- Connections are sensors — they tell you what the opponent is doing

---

## Common Mistakes

- Playing guard with no grips — legs engaged but hands doing nothing
- Gripping without purpose — holding fabric without directing force
- Clinging to a dead grip instead of transitioning to a new connection
- Ignoring lower body connections and trying to control with hands alone
- Having connections but not combining upper and lower for maximum leverage
- Not recognizing when the opponent is systematically stripping connections before a pass
- Using connections statically instead of actively directing force through them

## Application (Positional Examples)

### Closed Guard

- **Lower**: Legs locked around the waist — the primary structural connection
- **Upper**: Cross collar grip (posture break, choke entry) + sleeve grip (post control, arm isolation)
- **Combined play**: Break posture with collar pull, control sleeve to prevent posting, hips active for sweeps and submissions
- **If connection lost**: Legs unlock → transition to open guard and establish feet on hips immediately

### Butterfly Guard

- **Lower**: Both hooks inside the thighs — elevation and sweep platform
- **Upper**: Collar tie or underhook (direction) + sleeve or wrist grip (post control)
- **Combined play**: Pull with upper body while elevating with hooks — the sweep happens in the combination
- **If connection lost**: Hooks cleared → feet to hips, transition to open guard or seated guard

### De La Riva Guard

- **Lower**: DLR hook on the near leg, far foot on the hip or knee
- **Upper**: Same-side collar grip + far sleeve grip
- **Combined play**: DLR hook controls the base leg, collar pulls the upper body opposite → diagonal off-balance for sweeps and back takes
- **If connection lost**: DLR hook stripped → transition to shin-to-shin or reverse DLR depending on opponent's movement

### Half Guard

- **Lower**: Legs entangling one of the opponent's legs, knee shield maintaining space
- **Upper**: Underhook on the trapped-leg side (essential for sweeps) + far collar or lapel grip
- **Combined play**: Knee shield creates distance, underhook creates angle and connection for the sweep
- **If connection lost**: Underhook lost → fight for it immediately or transition to deep half or lockdown to maintain the guard

## Simple Mental Model

Puppet strings. Each grip and hook is a string connecting you to the opponent. The more strings you have, the more you can move them. Cut all the strings and the puppet walks away.

## Optional

### Drills

**Grip Circuit Drill**

- From closed guard, cycle through connections: collar grip → sleeve grip → overhook → underhook → collar tie
- For each, pull the opponent and feel what the grip does to their posture and balance
- Not about technique — about understanding what each connection controls

**Strip and Replace Drill**

- Start in open guard with two grips (e.g., collar and sleeve)
- Partner strips one grip
- You must establish a new connection within 2 seconds
- Partner then strips the new one — you replace again
- Continuous flow for 2 minutes. Builds the reflex of never being without connections.

**Blind Connection Drill**

- Close your eyes in closed guard or butterfly guard
- Maintain your connections while the partner moves slowly (posturing, shifting, trying to pass)
- Feel through your grips and hooks — identify what they are doing without looking
- Builds the habit of using connections as sensors

---

### Progression Notes

At this level, the focus is on identifying the key connection points for each guard position and understanding what each connection controls — direction, distance, structure, or leverage.

At higher levels, connection management becomes fluid and instinctive. Advanced guard players cycle through grips seamlessly, always maintaining at least two connections, always replacing what is stripped. Their grips are never static — they adjust with every movement. Connection management becomes the engine that makes complex guard systems like berimbolo, leg lock entries, and inverted guard possible. Every advanced guard technique is built on a specific set of connections.
